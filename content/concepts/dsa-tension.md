NYC-DSA endorsed Mamdani in October 2024 with overwhelming member support. The chapter's 8,800+ members formed the backbone of his campaign infrastructure — co-chairs met weekly with Mamdani and his campaign manager, and DSA formed dedicated task forces for fundraising, field operations, and communications. At the 2023 DSA national convention, Mamdani declared: "We are special as DSA electeds not because of ourselves; we are special because of our organization." After his general election win, DSA's national statement listed priorities: "a rent freeze, fast and free buses, and universal childcare... fight for a free Palestine, to get ICE out of our cities, fight for alternatives to policing."

Mamdani never downplayed his DSA membership — he quoted Eugene Debs in his victory speech. But PBS noted he said "he's running on his own distinct platform and does not embrace all of the activist group's priorities."

## Where He Departed from DSA Orthodoxy

The Jessica Tisch retention was the most visible breach. Retaining a billionaire-heiress police commissioner who opposed bail reform and supported expanding police ranks directly contradicted DSA's decarceration and police reform agenda. Eric Blanc argued in Jacobin that firing Tisch would "prompt a police (and potential capital) strike," essentially providing pragmatic cover. NYC-DSA co-chair Grace Mausser told Democracy Now: "We definitely expect there to still be challenges with a socialist mayor being in charge of the largest police force in the country."

The Hochul endorsement was the sharpest internal flashpoint. After winning, Mamdani endorsed centrist Governor Kathy Hochul — while she was actively using emergency powers to facilitate replacement labor during a historic nurses' strike. State Senator Jabari Brisport, a fellow DSA member, said: "Kathy Hochul is by billionaires, for billionaires... even Zohran gets it wrong sometimes." NYC-DSA issued a statement saying it "does not believe that Governor Kathy Hochul has risen to meet this moment" — but notably avoided a direct break with Mamdani.

The Trump meetings generated a different kind of discomfort. Mamdani met with Trump at the White House twice — and Trump called him "very rational." Critics from the World Socialist Web Site noted he had not posted a single social media item criticizing Trump since the meeting. He used the second meeting to pitch a $21 billion Sunnyside Yards development (12,000 affordable homes, 30,000 union jobs) and to raise the detention of a Columbia University student — but the optics of friendly engagement with someone he'd called "a fascist" generated left criticism.

NYPD arrests during the nurses' strike further strained the relationship. Under Mamdani's administration, NYPD arrested 13 nurses outside hospital association headquarters — while Mamdani himself had been appearing on picket lines. The contradiction was impossible to miss.

Budget austerity also cut against the grain. Despite running on taxing the rich, his early budget included cuts, a property tax hike proposal, and the CityFHEPS voucher reversal. The WSWS called this "the first open admission that his administration's priority is not meeting the urgent needs of the poor but balancing the budget."

## How the Left Reacted

The reaction mapped onto distinct left tendencies. Jacobin and DSA-aligned media offered critical solidarity, arguing these were necessary strategic compromises in building municipal socialism. Left Voice was sharper, calling the Hochul endorsement evidence that "socialist language loses its meaning when tied to a strategy of working within a party that exists to maintain capitalism." The WSWS was unsparing, running dozens of articles labeling his actions "betrayals" and describing him as "an operative of the ruling elite." DSA's institutional response was notably cautious — criticizing specific decisions without breaking with Mamdani, reflecting the organization's investment in his success.

## The CBC Questionnaire: Technocrat in Socialist Clothing?

In his Citizens Budget Commission questionnaire, Mamdani showed a strikingly pragmatic, technocratic tone — supporting mandatory Rainy Day Fund deposits, citywide performance management (akin to CompStat), and identifying DOE and DHS as needing efficiency improvements. He rated the city's fiscal transparency as "Poor." This was a different register entirely from his DSA-oriented messaging about decommodification and social ownership.

The tension is structural, not personal. A socialist mayor of a city with an $111 billion budget, 300,000 employees, and a $5.4 billion deficit is going to make decisions that look like standard liberal governance much of the time — because the machine doesn't stop running while you redesign it. The question DSA has to answer is whether holding the mayor's office under these constraints advances or dilutes the socialist project. The question Mamdani has to answer is whether the compromises are strategic retreats or permanent surrenders.
