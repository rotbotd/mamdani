Every politician breaks promises. What makes Mamdani's reversals worth tracking is that he ran the most detailed progressive platform in modern NYC history — specific dollar figures, timelines, mechanisms. The specificity makes departures visible in a way that vague campaign rhetoric doesn't.

## The Clear Reversals

**School governance.** Throughout the campaign, Mamdani positioned himself against mayoral control of schools, promoting a "co-governance" model that would empower parents, students, and educators through elected councils and local school teams. Then on December 31, 2025 — one day before inauguration — he announced he would seek to extend mayoral control, saying: "New Yorkers need to know where the buck stops: with me." Education advocate Leonie Haimson called it "very troubling." The timing was notable: announcing on New Year's Eve ensured minimal media coverage.

**CityFHEPS rental vouchers.** During the campaign, Mamdani explicitly promised to drop the city's lawsuit blocking expansion of CityFHEPS rental assistance vouchers and expand the program. As mayor, his administration moved in the opposite direction — his lawyers are negotiating to narrow the program's scope, citing budget constraints. City Councilwoman Tiffany Cabán criticized the shift publicly. This is the clearest broken promise of his first months in office.

## The Dramatic Evolution

**Policing** is where Mamdani moved furthest. In June 2020, he tweeted: "We don't need an investigation to know that the NYPD is racist, anti-queer & a major threat to public safety. What we need is to #DefundTheNYPD." He called for dismantling the department and described it as "wicked & corrupt." He laughed at a video of a police officer crying in his car.

By mid-2025, he had completely reversed: "I am not defunding the police. I am not running to defund the police." He publicly apologized to NYPD officers on Fox News for calling the department racist. He held private meetings with off-duty officers, obtained the endorsement of retired NYPD Chief of Department Rodney Harrison, and announced at the final debate that he would retain Commissioner Jessica Tisch — a billionaire heiress who opposed bail reform and supported expanding police ranks. The Justice Committee called the Tisch retention "a rebuff of his promises to New Yorkers and a disturbing endorsement of NYPD's ongoing violence and corruption."

As mayor, the contradictions sharpened further. He kept Tisch but canceled Adams' plan to hire 5,000 new officers and proposed a $22 million NYPD budget cut with 5,000 positions left vacant — prompting Republicans to accuse him of defunding the police after all. The Department of Community Safety has not yet appeared as a budget line item. When NYPD officers shot a 22-year-old Bangladeshi immigrant experiencing a mental health crisis in February 2026, Mamdani said DCS would be "hastened" but couldn't explain how it would handle cases involving weapons.

He also appeared to back down on the NYPD gangs database — pledging during the campaign to discontinue it but taking no action as of early 2026.

## The Quiet Shelving

Several promises have simply disappeared without formal reversal:

**Disbanding the Strategic Response Group.** A specific NYPD reform promise that appeared in policy documents and editorial board interviews but has not been mentioned since taking office.

**"Cop City" cancellation.** Pledged to cancel the proposed NYPD training facility. No action or further discussion since inauguration.

**Car-free streets in front of every school.** Mentioned in policy documents but not a visible priority in early governance.

**City-owned grocery stores.** The "public option for produce" was a distinctive campaign promise — one city-operated grocery store per borough as a pilot. Not mentioned in budget documents or public statements since inauguration.

**$30 minimum wage.** "30 by '30" was a headline labor promise. No legislative action or public push.

**Free CUNY tuition.** A major campaign commitment that requires Albany cooperation and significant revenue. Not addressed.

**Baby baskets.** Finnish-style maternity packages announced on WNYC in January 2025. Under $20 million — modest enough to implement easily. Has not appeared in any budget proposal.

## The Pattern

The reversals and shelved promises cluster around two categories. First, things that require Albany (free CUNY, $30 minimum wage, DCS funding) — these are constrained by the $5.4 billion deficit and Hochul's resistance to tax increases. Second, things that would generate intense political opposition without obvious constituency benefit (NYPD reforms, school governance changes) — these have been traded for pragmatic positioning.

What Mamdani has followed through on aggressively are things within mayoral executive authority that serve clear constituencies: housing executive orders, sanctuary protections, transit infrastructure, RGB appointments for the rent freeze. The pattern suggests a mayor who is genuinely progressive on substance but ruthlessly pragmatic about sequencing — doing what he can do alone first, deferring what requires allies he doesn't yet have.

Whether that's strategic patience or the beginning of permanent compromise is the central question of his administration.
