There are several streams and they're worth understanding because the way they currently work reveals a lot about who actually benefits from "affordable housing" policy.

## LIHTC

The biggest one is the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC). This is the dominant federal tool for affordable housing, responsible for roughly 90% of all affordable housing development nationally.

The way it works is genuinely convoluted but instructive. The federal government allocates tax credits to states, states allocate them to housing projects, and then developers sell those credits to private investors (banks and corporations mostly) who use them to reduce their federal tax bills. The investors give cash to the development in exchange for the tax reduction.

So right from the jump, the financing mechanism is structured as a tax shelter for wealthy investors. The housing gets built, sure, but the subsidy flows through landlords and a chunk of the value gets captured as investor returns and developer fees along the way.

There are two types: 9% credits, which are competitive and more generous, and 4% credits which are "as-of-right" and come bundled with tax-exempt bond financing. Both nonprofit and for-profit developers are eligible to apply.

That's important because it means the system already allows nonprofits to access these resources. The question is whether the city actively steers them toward nonprofits or keeps handing them to the same for-profit developers it has relationships with. Under de Blasio, the administration was so eager to hit its 200,000 unit target that it opted for the easiest route: providing subsidies to a few favorite for-profit affordable housing developers.

## Other Streams

Beyond LIHTC, there's:

- Direct city subsidy through HPD: loans to developers at below-market rates.

- Disposition of city-owned land: the city chooses who gets to build on public parcels through RFPs. This is a decision point where the city can prioritize nonprofits or for-profits.

- Housing Trust Fund: additional gap financing.

## Community Land Trusts

The community land trust angle is where this gets most interesting.

The Community Land Act currently before the City Council includes the Community Opportunity to Purchase Act, which would give CLTs and qualified nonprofits a first right to purchase multifamily buildings when landlords sell. There's also a Public Land for Public Good bill requiring the city to prioritize CLTs and nonprofits in public land dispositions.

That's a direct structural intervention: instead of public land going to for-profit developers who build "affordable" units with expiring affordability restrictions and extract profit throughout, the land goes into community ownership permanently.

## Why?

A nonprofit entity can qualify for the same tax credits as a for-profit developer. The financing tools are the same. The difference is that a nonprofit or CLT doesn't need to generate returns for investors on top of the development costs, so more of the subsidy actually goes into the housing.

And critically, a CLT takes the land permanently off the speculative market. You're not just building affordable housing that becomes market-rate in 30 years when the affordability restrictions expire. You're removing the land from the commodity circuit entirely.
