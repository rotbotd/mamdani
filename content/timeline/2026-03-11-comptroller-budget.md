---
title: "Comptroller Warns Budget Gap Worse Than Administration Projects"
date: 2026-03-11
tags:
  - budget
---

Comptroller Mark Levine released an assessment of the city's fiscal health ahead of the City Council's preliminary budget hearing, and it was grimmer than City Hall's own numbers. Where the Mamdani administration projected a $5.4 billion budget gap for next year, Levine's office put it at $6.5 billion — a difference that matters enormously when every proposed solution is already politically dead on arrival.

Mamdani's $127 billion preliminary budget proposed two paths for closing the gap: either the state legislature raises income taxes on the wealthiest New Yorkers and corporations, or the city raises property taxes by 9.5%. Governor Hochul has rejected income tax hikes, arguing they'd drive wealthy residents out of state. Council Speaker Menin called the property tax increase a "nonstarter." So neither path has the votes.

Levine flagged several concerns. The administration's revenue projections are too optimistic. CityFHEPS rental assistance alone is expected to cost $2.6 billion next year — more than HPD's entire expense budget. The proposed property tax increase approaches the constitutional ceiling, which means the city would be burning a tool it might need in a genuine crisis rather than a structural one. Levine also warned about geopolitical instability and the possibility that the AI stock market bubble could burst, causing major job losses.

One notable grace note: Levine praised Mamdani for honest budgeting. The Adams administration systematically underestimated expenses and "papered over" structural problems. Mamdani's budget at least reflects the true cost of key programs, which is how you end up with a number as alarming as $127 billion — it's not that spending exploded, it's that the previous mayor was lying about it.

The structural problem is that New York City is spending more money than it takes in during a period of economic growth and rising tax revenue. If the gap is $6.5 billion when things are going well, a recession would be catastrophic.
