title: "CityFHEPS Voucher Reversal Becomes Public"
date: 2026-03-03

---

The defining broken promise of Mamdani's first hundred days became undeniable on March 3 when Gothamist reported the full scope of his reversal on CityFHEPS rental vouchers.

During the campaign, Mamdani explicitly pledged to drop the Adams administration's legal challenge to a 2023 City Council package expanding CityFHEPS — the nation's largest municipal rental assistance program, currently covering over 65,000 households. The laws, passed over Adams' vetoes, would make more people facing eviction eligible and raise the income threshold to cover New Yorkers who earn slightly too much under current rules but still can't afford housing.

Instead of dropping the challenge, Mamdani's lawyers are negotiating to narrow the program's scope. His administration cited the $1.2 billion program's growing cost and the $5.4 billion budget gap. The city has until March 25 to file a challenge at the state Court of Appeals.

The fiscal case is real but uncomfortable. Comptroller Mark Levine projected CityFHEPS could cost $2.6 billion next year under its current form, and up to $20 billion over five years if the expansion laws are fully implemented. The program has grown from a pilot in 2018 to over $1 billion annually as rents surged and homelessness deepened — roughly 90,000 people are currently in city shelters.

The political dynamics are tangled. Behind closed doors, some advocates have floated compromises: expanding eligibility only to people in rent-stabilized apartments who can't afford even below-market rent, or capping total program spending (converting it from an entitlement to a first-come-first-served model like federal Section 8). Former Deputy Speaker Diana Ayala said she could live with the rent-stabilized expansion. But publicly, the Council and most advocacy organizations are demanding full implementation. Council Speaker Julie Menin: "The Council passed this law to be fully implemented, not endlessly litigated." Councilmember Pierina Sanchez, who sponsored two of the expansion bills: "There's a real argument to be made that it's more efficient and inexpensive to keep families permanently housed."

The federal context makes everything worse. The Trump administration is winding down a Biden-era emergency voucher program that covers nearly 8,000 NYC households. HUD is proposing two-year time limits on standard Section 8 assistance. New York State created a rental aid program last year but funded it with only $50 million and hasn't issued a single voucher.

VOCAL-NY housing campaigns director Adolfo Abreu: "We want them to commit to the full expansion and sustainably grow the program." Councilmember Crystal Hudson, who chairs general welfare: the Council "stands firm in its support of legislation that was passed."

The human cost is immediate. Juan Figueroa, 57, formerly homeless for over a decade, now faces eviction from the apartment he's lived in since 2017 because mental illness prevents him from working and he receives less than $200 a month in cash assistance. He doesn't qualify for CityFHEPS under current rules because he isn't in a DHS shelter. Under the expansion laws, he would. "I need help with the rent because I don't want to go back to the way I was," he told Gothamist. "I feel like a little mouse trapped in a corner with no way out."

This is the clearest test case for what happens when a socialist mayor hits fiscal reality. The campaign promise was unambiguous. The need is unambiguous. The budget gap is also unambiguous. Something has to give, and right now it's the people who were promised help.
