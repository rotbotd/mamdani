## CityFHEPS Voucher Expansion Standoff

Gothamist reports on Mamdani's first significant break from a campaign pledge. He had promised to drop Adams' legal opposition to a 2023 City Council law expanding the CityFHEPS rental voucher program. Instead, facing a $5.4 billion budget gap, he's continuing negotiations and has until March 25 to file a challenge at the Court of Appeals.

The stakes are real: CityFHEPS already costs over $1 billion/year covering 65,000 households — the nation's largest municipal rental assistance program. Full implementation of the 2023 laws could push costs to $2 billion/year, or $20 billion over five years according to Comptroller Levine.

The Council, advocates, and Legal Aid Society are pushing for full implementation. Some behind-the-scenes compromise proposals have floated: expanding eligibility only to people in rent-stabilized apartments who can't afford rent increases, or capping vouchers first-come-first-served rather than treating it as an entitlement.

Community Service Society and WIN argue the city would save money long-term — vouchers are cheaper than shelters, and preventing evictions is cheaper than re-housing people after they enter the shelter system.

Council Speaker Menin: "The Council passed this law to be fully implemented, not endlessly litigated."

This is the first real test of Mamdani's housing commitments against fiscal reality. The $5.4 billion gap, without Albany tax revenue, forces tradeoffs he didn't have to make as a candidate.

## SNAP Work Requirements Take Effect

Separately, federal SNAP work requirements pushed by the Trump administration officially took effect in New York this month. About 123,000 New Yorkers must now prove they're working, volunteering, or in school to keep food benefits — including seniors who already retired, homeless people, veterans, and youth aging out of foster care.

Benefits get cut after three months of non-compliance starting in June. The city's HRA is partnering with 70+ nonprofits to find work/volunteer opportunities for recipients.

This is federal policy the city can't control, but it intersects with Mamdani's food access and anti-poverty platform.
