## First 2-K Neighborhoods Announced

Mayor Mamdani and Governor Hochul unveiled the first phase of universal child care for 2-year-olds. Four high-need areas across four boroughs will get about 2,000 free seats starting September 2026:

School District 6 (Washington Heights, Hamilton Heights, Inwood), District 10 (Fordham, Belmont, Norwood, Morris Heights, Van Cortlandt Village, Kingsbridge), Districts 18 and 23 (Canarsie, Remsen Village, Brownsville, Ocean Hill), and District 27 (Ozone Park, South Ozone Park, Richmond Hill, Howard Beach, Woodhaven, Rockaways).

Hochul committed $73 million for year one, with $425 million planned for year two to expand to 12,000 seats across all five boroughs by fall 2027. The eventual goal is free child care for any NYC 2-year-old regardless of income, ZIP code, or immigration status.

The rollout relies heavily on existing child care infrastructure, particularly home-based providers. No plans to use school buildings — officials say child care centers and family providers are "most developmentally appropriate" for toddlers. Rolling admissions will accommodate kids turning 2 from September through December.

Open questions remain: whether programs will offer a full 6-hour-20-minute day, and how to address the chronic underpayment of child care workers who "earn less than 96% of other professions" according to the Mayor's Office of Child Care.

Mamdani: "Raising a child takes a village — and it takes a city government willing to step up and tackle the child care crisis head-on."

This is the first concrete delivery on his signature child care promise.

## CityFHEPS Voucher Expansion Standoff

Gothamist reports on Mamdani's first significant break from a campaign pledge. He had promised to drop Adams' legal opposition to a 2023 City Council law expanding the CityFHEPS rental voucher program. Instead, facing a $5.4 billion budget gap, he's continuing negotiations and has until March 25 to file a challenge at the Court of Appeals.

The stakes are real: CityFHEPS already costs over $1 billion/year covering 65,000 households — the nation's largest municipal rental assistance program. Full implementation of the 2023 laws could push costs to $2 billion/year, or $20 billion over five years according to Comptroller Levine.

The Council, advocates, and Legal Aid Society are pushing for full implementation. Some behind-the-scenes compromise proposals have floated: expanding eligibility only to people in rent-stabilized apartments who can't afford rent increases, or capping vouchers first-come-first-served rather than treating it as an entitlement.

Community Service Society and WIN argue the city would save money long-term — vouchers are cheaper than shelters, and preventing evictions is cheaper than re-housing people after they enter the shelter system.

Council Speaker Menin: "The Council passed this law to be fully implemented, not endlessly litigated."

This is the first real test of Mamdani's housing commitments against fiscal reality. The $5.4 billion gap, without Albany tax revenue, forces tradeoffs he didn't have to make as a candidate.

## New Public Toilets While 50 Park Bathrooms Sit Closed

THE CITY reports on a classic governance tension: Mamdani announced a $4 million initiative to build 20-30 new modular public toilets in plazas and commercial corridors, while roughly 50 existing park bathrooms remain closed for repairs — some for years.

The Parks Department says 90% of its ~1,000 bathrooms are operational, but the 10% that aren't have become chronic problems. At Riverside Park alone, 5 of 13 public restrooms are closed due to broken water mains, lack of heat, and electrical issues. Some haven't had interior upgrades in 70-80 years.

The procurement system is also a mess. Councilmember Mark Levine secured $1.8 million for a modular restroom in Fort Washington Park in July 2021 — five years later, it's still in procurement, nowhere near construction.

Councilmember Gale Brewer: "As the mayor is talking about spending money on new bathrooms, we do need to fix the ones that exist."

This intersects with the parks funding promise — Mamdani pledged 1% of the budget for Parks, but his preliminary budget keeps funding essentially flat at about half that. The final budget is due July 1.

## SNAP Work Requirements Take Effect

Separately, federal SNAP work requirements pushed by the Trump administration officially took effect in New York this month. About 123,000 New Yorkers must now prove they're working, volunteering, or in school to keep food benefits — including seniors who already retired, homeless people, veterans, and youth aging out of foster care.

Benefits get cut after three months of non-compliance starting in June. The city's HRA is partnering with 70+ nonprofits to find work/volunteer opportunities for recipients.

This is federal policy the city can't control, but it intersects with Mamdani's food access and anti-poverty platform.

## Council Slams Adams-Era Transit Failures

At a City Council hearing, Mamdani's new DOT Commissioner faced sharp criticism over the Adams administration's failure to meet bus and bike lane mandates. The city hit only about 50% of legally required protected lane miles in recent years.

Councilmember Lincoln Restler: "A 50% is a big, fat F."

The new commissioner promised to step up installation rates, framing it as cleaning up Adams' mess. The Local Law 195 mandate requires specific mileage targets for protected bus and bike lanes annually. Adams consistently fell short while blaming community opposition and coordination challenges.

This is an easy political win for Mamdani — blame the predecessor, promise to do better — but actual delivery will require navigating the same community board politics that stalled Adams. The difference may be political will: Mamdani ran explicitly on car-free streets and transit priority, while Adams never seemed to care much either way.
