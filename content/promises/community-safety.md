Mamdani's single biggest policy proposal: a new $1.1 billion civilian-led department taking a public health approach to safety. About $605 million would come from consolidating existing programs, $455 million in new funding. The basic theory is that NYPD currently handles roughly 180,000 calls per year involving emotionally disturbed individuals, and cops are the wrong tool for that job.

## The Five Pillars

**Gun violence.** A 275% increase to the Crisis Management System (the city's Cure Violence-model program). CMS currently operates in only 29 of 77 precincts and only works five days a week, eight hours a day — most violence happens during hours they're not even on the clock.

**Subway safety.** 100-station outreach teams and "stabilization centers" in empty MTA retail spaces, giving people in crisis somewhere to go that isn't a platform or train car.

**Mental health crises.** Citywide expansion of B-HEARD, which currently still operates with limited capacity and is unable to respond to about a third of eligible calls within its pilot area. The proposal also includes Community Mental Health Navigators embedded in neighborhoods.

**Homelessness.** Transit outreach teams, integrated with mental health and housing services rather than operating as separate silos.

**Hate crimes.** An 800% increase in hate crime prevention measures.

The model Mamdani references is CAHOOTS in Eugene, Oregon — 35 years operating without serious injury to staff. But Eugene has 175,000 people. Scaling that to a city of 8 million with 180,000 EDP calls a year is a fundamentally different engineering problem.

## Does He Have the Power?

The mayor has authority over city agencies. He appoints commissioners, sets budgets, reorganizes executive branch operations. He can expand B-HEARD, increase CMS funding, and beef up the existing Mayor's Office of Criminal Justice through executive action.

But creating a whole new *department* — as opposed to expanding an existing office — several observers believe City Council legislation would be legally necessary. Council Member Lincoln Restler introduced legislation in December 2025, reintroduced in January 2026 because of the new Council session. The bill language is deliberately broad, giving the administration flexibility.

The bill had 27 co-sponsors originally but lost four when those members left the body. More importantly, it's unclear if the bill has support from Council Speaker Julie Menin, a moderate Democrat whose leadership bid Mamdani did not support. Personal political friction layered on structural questions.

The administration says it is "pursuing all options," which has led to speculation they might instead pursue a beefed-up version of an existing office rather than a new department. This matters because the Council route gives permanence (harder for the next mayor to kill) and political legitimacy, but also gives Menin leverage.

## The Budget Problem

Funding for DCS is notably absent from the preliminary budget released February 2026. The $127 billion budget keeps NYPD at essentially the same $6.4 billion while canceling Adams' plan to hire 5,000 additional officers. Mamdani says DCS funding will appear in the executive budget revision before July 1.

But he's working against a $5.4 billion gap. His proposed revenue solutions — raising income taxes on high earners and corporate taxes at the state level, or a 9.5% property tax increase if Albany doesn't cooperate — have Menin, Comptroller Levine, and Hochul all publicly opposed.

## Institutional Design Questions

Mamdani is planning to hire a new deputy mayor by spring to oversee DCS. The department would report through a new Deputy Mayor for Community Safety rather than being subordinate to the existing Deputy Mayor for Public Safety (who oversees NYPD). That structural positioning matters — it puts civilian safety response at the same bureaucratic rank as policing rather than underneath it.

Richard Aborn of the Citizens Crime Commission worries that a standalone "department" might lack the political teeth necessary to force siloed city agencies to collaborate. He argues the administration needs a deputy mayor with authority to command resources from police, housing, and education agencies. This echoes findings about de Blasio-era programs: one agency simply does not possess the authority to secure commitments, resources, and prioritized attention from another in the same way a mayoral unit does.

Talib Hudson from The New Hood raises a different concern: avoid giving DCS too much responsibility without commensurate resources. "Someone who is experiencing a mental health episode or suffering from an opioid addiction on the subway is not necessarily the same as someone who is trapped in a cycle of violence." Bundling five very different problems under one roof could mean none of them get the specific, sustained attention they need.

## Where It Stands

He can expand existing programs through executive action right now. He can hire people, reorganize offices, redirect funding within existing budgets. Creating the full department requires Council legislation that may face resistance from Menin. Funding it at the proposed $1.1 billion scale requires either Albany cooperation on tax increases (Hochul opposed) or politically explosive property tax hikes (Menin and Levine opposed).

And even if he gets the department created and funded, the actual operational challenge of making siloed agencies cooperate in real time — the thing that would actually make it work for the person in crisis on the subway platform — is the hardest part, and it's the part that no legislation or budget line automatically solves.
