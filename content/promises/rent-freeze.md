A four-year freeze on rents for all ~1 million rent-stabilized apartments, affecting roughly 2 million tenants. Mamdani's single most identifiable campaign promise.

## How It Works

New York City's Rent Guidelines Board votes annually on how much landlords can raise rents on rent-stabilized apartments. The board has nine members, all appointed by the mayor: two represent tenants, two represent landlords, and five are "public members" who are supposed to be neutral. In practice the public members determine the outcome, and in practice the mayor determines who the public members are.

This is unusual. In most cities, rent regulation is set by state legislatures or formulas tied to inflation. In New York, the mayor has near-total control over the body that decides rent increases for a million apartments. The RGB doesn't need City Council approval, state legislative action, or any other branch of government to act. The mayor appoints the board, the board votes, and that's the rent increase for the next lease cycle.

The catch is that board terms are staggered. Members serve two-year terms on overlapping schedules, so a new mayor can't sweep the entire board on Day 1. Normally it takes a full year before a mayor can appoint a majority. This creates an intentional buffer against sudden policy shifts — the board was designed to be somewhat insulated from electoral politics, even though the appointment power makes that insulation illusory over time.

The RGB process runs on a fixed annual calendar. Staff economists publish research reports in the spring. The board holds a preliminary vote (usually April or May) setting a range of possible increases. Public hearings follow, where tenants and landlords testify. Then a final vote in June sets the actual numbers for leases beginning October 1. The board can vote for an increase, a freeze (0%), or technically even a rollback (negative increase), though rollbacks have never happened.

In the board's entire history, it has voted for a freeze exactly four times: 2015 and 2016 under de Blasio (one-year leases only), and 2020 during COVID. The de Blasio freezes were controversial even within his own appointees — the 2015 freeze passed 5-4, with one public member dissenting. A four-year freeze would be completely unprecedented.

## The Adams Blockade

On his way out, Adams tried to insulate the board from Mamdani. In December 2025, Adams rushed through four new RGB appointments — filling seats that would normally have been left for the incoming mayor. The strategy was to lock in a pro-landlord majority for Mamdani's first year, denying him the votes for a freeze in June 2026.

The legal basis was thin. Adams was a lame duck who had lost re-election in a landslide, and the appointments were transparently designed to override the incoming administration's mandate. But the appointments were technically legal — there's no rule preventing a mayor from filling board vacancies during a transition.

What Adams didn't anticipate was attrition. One of his December appointees dropped out almost immediately. By mid-February, resignations had opened up six of nine seats. On February 18, 2026, Mamdani made six appointments in a single day, including new chair Chantella Mitchell. This gave him a commanding majority well ahead of the June vote.

The new appointees include tenant advocates and housing researchers. Mitchell, the new chair, is a housing policy researcher who has published work on rent stabilization's effects on neighborhood stability. The tenant-side appointees include organizers from housing advocacy groups. The landlord-side appointees are required by law but don't determine outcomes.

## The Conservative Legal Challenge

The Manhattan Institute published a preemptive legal argument in December 2025 claiming a rent freeze would violate the board's statutory mandate. Their argument: the RGB is required by law to consider landlords' ability to maintain buildings and earn a "reasonable return," and a freeze would constitute an arbitrary and capricious failure to weigh those factors.

This is the argument that would show up in court if landlords sue. The RGB's enabling statute says the board must consider "the economic condition of the residential real estate industry," including operating costs, vacancy rates, and whether owners are maintaining their properties. A freeze, critics argue, ignores rising costs (insurance, property taxes, water/sewer rates) and effectively confiscates landlord income.

The counterargument is that the same RGB data shows landlord profits rising 12% annually even during prior freeze years. If net operating income is increasing despite cost pressures, a freeze isn't confiscatory — it just slows the rate of profit growth. The RGB has the discretion to weigh these factors however it sees fit, and courts have historically deferred to the board's judgment. The 2015 and 2016 freezes both survived legal challenges.

The deeper issue is that "reasonable return" has never been defined by the courts. Is it a percentage? A dollar amount? Enough to cover costs plus some margin? The ambiguity has always worked in landlords' favor because the board has traditionally erred toward increases. A freeze-oriented board could simply define "reasonable return" differently — landlords are still profitable, just less profitable than they'd like.

## Differential Guidelines

One of the more interesting details of Mamdani's rent proposal is the concept of differential guidelines. Rather than a single number applied to all buildings, buildings with the worst code violations would receive zero increases regardless — even if other buildings get small ones. The idea is that landlords who don't maintain their buildings shouldn't benefit from the same rent increase as landlords who do.

This has been discussed at the RGB for years but never implemented. The enforcement mechanism would be tied to HPD's building violation database. Buildings above some threshold of open violations (heat complaints, lead paint, structural issues) would be automatically excluded from any increase. This creates a direct financial incentive for landlords to fix problems — if violations prevent you from raising rent, fixing them becomes an investment rather than a cost.

The complication is that some buildings have violations precisely because the landlord can't afford repairs. A freeze combined with differential guidelines could create a death spiral for the most distressed buildings: no rent increase means less revenue, less revenue means fewer repairs, fewer repairs means more violations, more violations means continued freeze. The hardship exemption process is supposed to catch these cases, but the process is slow and landlords complain it's bureaucratic.

## What Happens in June

The June 2026 vote is now the most watched RGB vote in decades. With Mamdani's six appointees holding a commanding majority, a freeze appears virtually certain. The question is whether the board votes for a full freeze on both one-year and two-year leases, or a partial freeze (0% on one-year leases, small increase on two-year leases as in 2015-2016).

A full freeze would be the strongest statement. A partial freeze would give the board more legal defensibility in case of a court challenge — they could point to the two-year lease increase as evidence they considered landlord costs.

The preliminary vote is expected in April or May. Public hearings will follow. The final vote is typically in late June. Leases affected would be those beginning on or after October 1, 2026.

If the freeze holds for four consecutive years as Mamdani promised, it would cover lease cycles through 2029-2030. That would require Mamdani to maintain his board majority through his entire first term and for no appointees to defect. Given that he controls all appointments and the board only needs a simple majority, this seems likely — unless a court intervenes.

## The Albany Problem

Mamdani acknowledged during the campaign that a rent freeze puts pressure on landlords, and promised to offset this by pushing Albany to lower property taxes and insurance costs for building owners. This is the part of the promise that depends on the state, not the city.

Property taxes are set by the city, but the assessment system is governed by state law. New York City's property tax system is notoriously broken — a co-op in Manhattan can pay ten times the effective rate of a single-family home in Staten Island with the same market value. Fixing this requires state legislation, and every previous attempt has died in Albany because homeowner constituencies in the outer boroughs benefit from the current system's distortions.

Insurance costs are even harder. Commercial property insurance in New York has been rising 15-25% annually, driven by climate risk repricing, litigation costs, and a hard reinsurance market. The city and state have essentially no leverage over insurance pricing. Mamdani could push for a state-backed insurance pool for rent-stabilized buildings, but this hasn't been proposed in any detail.

The honest assessment is that the Albany side of this promise is aspirational. The rent freeze itself is within Mamdani's power. The cost offsets for landlords are not.
