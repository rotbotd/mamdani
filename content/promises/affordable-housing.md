200,000 new affordable homes over ten years. All permanently affordable, rent-stabilized, built with 100% union labor. Target: families earning under $70,000/year.

## What

The city already has programs that fund affordable housing construction. HPD runs SARA (Senior Affordable Rental Apartments) for low-income seniors and ELLA (Extremely Low and Low-Income Affordability) for the poorest families. These programs give developers cheap loans and tax breaks in exchange for rent caps. Mamdani would scale these up significantly.

The bigger move is a new Social Housing Development Agency modeled on Vienna's system. Vienna's model is famous: the city owns or subsidizes about 60% of all housing, rents are roughly a third of what they are in comparable European cities, and middle-income residents live alongside low-income ones so the buildings don't become warehouses for the poor. In this model, the city also acts as a developer in that it builds housing directly and keeps it permanently off the speculative market.

## Money

Price tag: $100 billion over ten years.

$30 billion would come from existing capital commitments, basically money the city already plans to spend on housing. The other $70 billion requires issuing new municipal bonds. Basically just direct borrowing money without the bank.

Here's the problem: NYC has a debt ceiling set by Albany. The city can't just borrow unlimited money. Recently, Chicago, Los Angeles, San Francisco, and Washington all got their credit ratings downgraded. Nationally, cities hold $1.4 trillion in debt, and state and local governments combined owe over $6.1 trillion. On a per capita basis, New York, Connecticut, New Jersey, Illinois, and Hawaii all exceed $30,000 per person in state and local debt.

Mamdani would need the state legislature to raise that ceiling, which means convincing Albany that $70 billion in new housing debt is worth the risk. Which, considering the climate, seems pretty dangerous. Albany has its own budget fights and isn't automatically friendly to city priorities, even with a Democratic supermajority.

Additional context: Trump cancelled almost $7.6 billion in climate and energy grants authorized by the Inflation Reduction Act, targeting 321 awards supporting 223 projects, almost entirely in states that voted for Harris. They've also cancelled $1.5 billion in DOT and CDC grants to California, Colorado, Illinois, and Minnesota, and paused $2.1 billion in Chicago transit modernization projects pending review of what they called "race-based contracting." So, that makes matters much worse for the states and cities already drowning in debt.

At some point you have to ask: what's the credit rating going to look like when the federal government is actively trying to defund you? Four major cities got credit downgrades in a five-month span. A downgrade raises borrowing costs, which means the same $70 billion in bonds now costs taxpayers more in interest payments, which means more future revenue gets redirected to bondholders instead of services. Yuck.

## Mid-Campaign Pivot

A notable evolution: Mamdani told the New York Times that housing was the one issue where he'd changed his mind: he came to recognize the importance of the private sector in solving the crisis. By October 2025, he told ABNY his 200,000 city-subsidized homes would be "additive" to private market construction, not a replacement. Vital City described this as a "soft-launched technocratic pivot."

Worth watching whether the governing reality looks more like the early socialist framing or the later "additive" framing.
