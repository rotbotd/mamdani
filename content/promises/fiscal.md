The revenue engine for Mamdani's entire platform — and the constraint that explains why most of it hasn't happened yet.

## The Tax Proposals

**Income tax surcharge.** A 2% surcharge on NYC residents earning over $1 million, raising the effective rate from ~3.88% to ~5.88%. Projected to generate ~$4 billion/year.

**Corporate tax increase.** Raise the corporate tax from 7.25% to 11.5% (matching New Jersey). Projected to generate ~$5–7 billion/year.

Both require Albany approval. The revenue was earmarked for free CUNY, childcare, free buses, and tenant protections.

**Property tax reform.** Correct the system where luxury developments pay lower effective rates than working-class outer-borough homeowners.

In June 2025, Mamdani stated flatly: "I don't think we should have billionaires."

## The $5.4 Billion Problem

The gap between Mamdani's ambitions and his early governance is explained almost entirely by the $5.4 billion budget deficit he inherited. His platform depended on Albany approving massive new taxes. Governor Hochul has rejected the tax increases. The state legislature hasn't acted.

Without that revenue — roughly $9–11 billion/year from the income surcharge and corporate tax hike — the math for free buses, universal childcare, free CUNY, city grocery stores, and the Department of Community Safety simply doesn't work.

## The Property Tax Threat

Facing the deficit, Mamdani proposed a 9.5% property tax increase as a "last resort" if Albany refuses the income and corporate tax hikes. Council Speaker Menin opposes it. Comptroller Levine opposes it. Hochul is noncommittal.

The proposal reads as a pressure tactic: give me the millionaire's tax, or I'll impose a deeply unpopular property tax that you'll get blamed for too.

## What It Means

Housing and immigration protections are achievable through executive power and existing mechanisms — RGB appointments, executive orders, land-use authority. Childcare got a partial win through the Hochul alliance. Everything else is in a holding pattern.

The most honest assessment: Mamdani has been remarkably aggressive on things a mayor can do unilaterally while the transformative fiscal and programmatic agenda awaits revenue that may never come.

## The CBC Questionnaire

In his Citizens Budget Commission questionnaire, Mamdani showed a strikingly pragmatic, technocratic tone — supporting mandatory Rainy Day Fund deposits, citywide performance management (akin to CompStat), and identifying DOE and DHS as needing efficiency improvements. He rated the city's fiscal transparency as "Poor."

This was a different register entirely from his DSA-oriented messaging about decommodification and social ownership.
