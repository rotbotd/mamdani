Reform the city's land use process to build more housing, especially in wealthy neighborhoods that have historically blocked density.

## City of Yes

Under Adams, the City Council passed "City of Yes for Housing Opportunity" in December 2024, which is a citywide upzoning that allows more housing near transit, legalizes accessory dwelling units, removes parking minimums, and other reforms. It was the biggest zoning change in decades.

Mamdani supported it but said it "didn't go far enough." Too many carve-outs protected low-density neighborhoods, particularly wealthier ones. The result is that the housing gets built where there's already density (and political weakness to resist it) rather than where it's most needed or where there's the most transit access.

He cited Jersey City and Tokyo as models. Jersey City has been building aggressively and seen rents stabilize. Tokyo famously builds enough housing to keep prices flat despite being one of the world's largest cities, primarily because zoning is set at the national level and localities can't block development.

## Comprehensive City Plan

Mamdani proposed a Comprehensive City Plan to reform what he called the city's "disjointed" land use process. Right now, development happens project-by-project through ULURP (Uniform Land Use Review Procedure), which lets local actors extract concessions or kill projects entirely. A comprehensive plan would set citywide goals and reduce the power of individual community boards to block housing.

## Who Gets to Build

For projects that commit to affordability, union labor, and sustainability, fast-tracked planning review.

No more tax breaks for luxury condo projects. Moratorium on rezonings that result in net loss of affordable units.

Redirecting [[concepts/housing-subsidies|resources]] toward nonprofit developers over for-profit ones. The idea is that nonprofit developers (community development corporations, land trusts) don't need to generate returns for investors, so more of the subsidy goes into the housing rather than profit.

## Community Land Trusts and Limited-Equity Cooperatives

Mamdani supports community land trusts (CLTs) and limited-equity cooperatives.

A community land trust is a nonprofit that owns land permanently and leases it to homeowners or renters. When you sell, you can only sell at a formula price, not market rate. This keeps housing affordable across generations rather than letting appreciation get captured by whoever happens to own it when prices spike.

A limited-equity cooperative is similar: residents own shares in the building collectively, but resale prices are capped. You build equity slowly rather than gambling on the housing market.

Both models take housing out of the speculative market entirely. The tradeoff is that individual residents don't get the windfall gains that homeownership normally provides, but that's exactly the point. Homes should not be an investment. They should be for housing. Yes, those are mutually exclusive.
