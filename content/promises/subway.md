Supported a fare freeze at $2.75 (fares rose to $3.00 on January 4, 2026). Proposed mental health outreach teams at 100 subway stations, converting abandoned MTA retail spaces into stabilization centers. Pushed for six-minute train headways and 20% service increases. Provided free OMNY cards for CUNY students. Supported Second Avenue Subway Phase 2.

## Problem

The mayor doesn't control the subway. The MTA is a state agency run by the governor. This is the same constraint as [[promises/free-buses|free buses]]: the city's residents depend on a transit system that city government doesn't run.

## Mental health outreach

This is where the mayor has the most direct authority.

Mamdani proposed creating a Department of Community Safety that would station teams of three "outreach specialists" (peers with lived experience, mental health professionals, and EMTs) at 100 subway stations prioritized by rates of homelessness and reported incidents. Total cost: $60 million. Mayor can do this without Albany.

Adams created the PATH program, which deploys outreach workers alongside NYPD officers. If a nurse declares someone a risk, police officers bring them to the hospital for involuntary evaluation for up to 72 hours, whether or not the person wants to go.

Mamdani wants to remove police from that equation entirely. His campaign said "the Department of Community Safety will create new outreach teams of peers, mental health specialists, and EMTs trained to support people who are homeless or struggling with mental illness" without NYPD involvement. The argument is that police presence agitates people in crisis and that peer-led programs are proven to create better long-term outcomes.

### "Not safe without police!"

So this "safety" framing completely inverts the actual problem. The question isn't "is it safe for outreach workers to approach homeless people without armed escorts?" The question is "why are thousands of people with severe mental illness living in subway stations in the first place?"

How it works right now:

The PATH model puts four police officers with one nurse and two outreach coordinators on each team. Four cops per team. One PATH outreach worker, Shawn Tish, who is formerly homeless himself, said the police presence sometimes makes people hesitant to engage, but added "they're there for us, for our protection" and typically only intervene if someone tries to attack a member of the team. The Adams administration's framing was that "the presence of police affords clinicians a greater sense of personal safety, enabling more meaningful engagement with those in need."

So the argument for police is basically: outreach workers feel safer with cops around, and cops can forcibly transport people to hospitals when a nurse determines someone is a danger to themselves or others. In the first six months, PATH teams interacted with 11,000 people, 144 of which ended in police initiating an involuntary hospital transport. That's about 1.3% of contacts resulting in forced removal.

Problem: the teams referred more than 1,000 individuals to "safe settings" like shelters or drop-in centers, but they don't track how many actually showed up. They don't track what happens after someone is involuntarily taken to a hospital either.

City Hall declined to provide an update on involuntary hospital transports, and existing data has failed to capture what happens after someone is taken to the hospital. So the whole accountability chain is broken. You _can't evaluate_ whether police involvement actually produces better outcomes because nobody is measuring the outcomes.

The Comptroller's report found that the city spends approximately $300 million on outreach programs annually but fails to ensure these programs have the technology needed to track engagements, and routinely fails to notify outreach workers when clients are admitted to or discharged from hospitals and jails. Such as:

In 2024, outreach teams secured supportive housing approvals for 955 unsheltered people, but only 175, just 18%, actually obtained a unit, often due to process barriers. At the same time, more than 4,100 supportive housing units sat vacant. **Four thousand vacant supportive housing units while people sleep in subway stations.** The system can't connect people to housing that literally exists and is sitting empty.

So what is causing homelessness? Decades of defunding public housing, gutting mental health services, treating housing as a commodity rather than a right, and then responding to the visible consequences of all that disinvestment with policing rather than with the housing and care that would actually solve the problem.

Look, the police are there because it's cheaper and more politically palatable to manage homelessness through force than to provide the housing and services that would end it. There are approximately 2,000 people with serious mental illness cycling through streets, subways, jails, and hospitals. I mean, seriously? Why can't we house 2,000 people? Is it really that hard?

## Vacant subway retail spaces (negotiation)

A clever move. Mamdani proposed converting abandoned MTA commercial spaces into drop-in hubs for counseling, first aid, and basic needs, modeled on Philadelphia's Hub of Hope — an 11,000 square foot facility that serves about 127 people a day on a $2.5 million budget.

The MTA owns these spaces and many are sitting empty. Mamdani estimated this would cost $10 million. He needs MTA cooperation for the physical spaces, but the city would staff and fund the services. So it's a negotiation rather than a dependency.

## Fare affordability (partially controlled)

The mayor can't set subway fares, but he controls the city's Fair Fares program, which currently gives half-priced transit to low-income New Yorkers.

Advocates are pushing Mamdani to expand Fair Fares to provide completely free transit for people making up to 150% of the federal poverty level and half-priced fares for those making up to 300%. That would cover over two million New Yorkers.

But the biggest hurdle is a $2 billion city budget deficit this year and a $10 billion gap next year.

The CUNY OMNY cards are a smaller version of the same principle: the city subsidizes transit for a specific population it has a relationship with.

## Service increases and headways (MTA)

Getting this out of the way, **headways** are just the time between trains. If the headway is six minutes, that means a train comes every six minutes. If it's twelve minutes, you're standing on the platform twice as long. "Service increases" just means running more trains more often.

These are almost entirely MTA decisions. The mayor can advocate, he has appointees on the MTA board, and he has a bully pulpit. But he can't order six-minute headways.

What he can do is support [[promises/congestion-pricing|congestion pricing]] revenue flowing into service improvements, which is already happening with the $15 billion in bonded capital projects, and push the MTA to prioritize frequency over other spending.

## "Parking meter argument"

One Vital City analysis made a provocative argument that Mamdani could make both buses AND subways free if the city metered all its parking spots. This could generate $3.8 billion annually at an average of $3.47 per spot per day, which is enough to cover the approximately $3.8 billion the MTA would give up by ending all fares.

That's a transfer from car owners to transit riders, the same redistributive logic as congestion pricing but much more aggressive.
