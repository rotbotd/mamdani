Committed to implementing the 2022 class size mandate (caps of 20–25 students by 2028). Proposed a $12,000/year tuition assistance program for new teachers. Named Kamar Samuels as schools chancellor.

This is a $43 billion system with roughly 150,000 staff and nearly 900,000 students. Nearly 45% of students in grades 3-8 are below grade level. Roughly 154,000 students are homeless.

## Trump (digression)

On September 16, 2025, federal education officials informed NYC that magnet school funding was in jeopardy because they were "deeply concerned" with city policies allowing transgender students to participate in sports and use bathrooms in line with their gender identity. The federal Department of Education claimed these policies violate Title IX. They gave the city until the following Tuesday, just one week, to agree to overhaul its transgender student policies or lose $36 million in grants spread over the next three fiscal years.

This is flatly grotesque. Transgender students have nothing to do with this federal funding. There are 6,000-10,000 of them in total across entire NYC school system. Trump is making threats that apply to all students that attend these schools based on an irrelevant concern about less than a percantage point out of a million. That is hostage-taking. That is straight up dystopian cruelty. Any pretext that the media was right to remain "objective" and frame the situation as just "withholding funding" is dishonest garbage.

## Class Size Mandate

A state law passed in 2022 requires NYC to cap class sizes at 20 students in lower grades and 25 in high schools, with 80% of classes meeting those targets by next school year and 100% compliance by 2027-28.

Problem: just under 65% of classrooms met the requirement, and the city had to grant a significant number of exemptions to get even that far.

So the administration needs to hire more than 10,000 new educators on top of the 75,000 they already have, and needs up to $700 million in additional funding. Samuels testified that meeting just the 80% target requires 6,000 additional teachers at a cost of $602 million.

Adams constantly complained this was an unfunded mandate and would cost $15 billion in construction alone. Mamdani has refused to ask Albany to pause or scale back the law.

## New teachers?

The state's new teacher pipeline is drying up at exactly the moment the city needs a massive hiring surge, particularly in hard-to-staff positions like special education, bilingual education, math, and science.

Mamdani's proposed solution: $12,000/year tuition assistance for college students who commit to teaching in NYC schools for at least three years. Starting with 1,000 students at about $12 million a year, funded by cutting extraneous contracts within the DOE.

Mamdani acknowledged this won't meet the city's full teacher need but said he hopes to expand the program and improve retention of existing teachers.

## Labor

Backed the UFT-supported RESPECT check (~$10,000) for paraprofessionals.

Pledged to make fixing Tier 6 pension a key Albany priority. Tier 6 is the pension tier for public employees hired after 2012. It has significantly worse benefits than earlier tiers, creating a two-tier workforce doing the same job with different retirement security.

Smaller class sizes are themselves a labor question. You need more teachers, which means more hiring, better pay, better working conditions.

## Mayoral Control Reversal

Mamdani campaigned on ending mayoral control of schools, a system that's given the NYC mayor nearly complete power over the public school system for more than 20 years.

But at the press conference announcing Samuels, he reversed course and said he'll ask Albany to extend it, while promising to make it more participatory: restructuring parent meetings so working parents can attend, improving awareness of community education councils where voter turnout is less than 2%, and making parent coordinators actual organizers rather than administrative roles.

Pragmatic pivot or broken promise, depending on how you look at it.

## Gifted and Talented

So Mamdani's stance on this issue is basically about eliminating the kindergarten G&T admission track while preserving programs beginning in 3rd grade. His spokesperson said "identifying academic giftedness at age 4 is hard to do objectively by any assessment." This affected ~2,500 of 55,000 kindergartners.

Now the obvious question is, why would you ever try to assess "giftedness" in someone who hasn't learned to read yet?

The short answer is it's a sorting mechanism that has almost nothing to do with identifying "giftedness" and almost everything to do with giving middle-class and upper-middle-class families a reason to stay in the public school system instead of fleeing to private schools.

The history is basically this: NYC has always had some version of tracked programs, but the modern G&T system got formalized and expanded significantly under Bloomberg in the late 2000s. Before that it was a patchwork, district-by-district thing. Bloomberg standardized it with a single citywide test for four-year-olds, the Gifted & Talented admissions exam, which was basically an IQ-style assessment administered to preschoolers. The explicit logic was: if we can offer a "premium" track within public schools, families who might otherwise go private will stay, and that keeps their property tax dollars and political engagement in the public system.

The problem is obvious the second you think about it for five minutes. What does a standardized test of a four-year-old actually measure? It measures whether the kid has parents who can afford test prep. And test prep for four-year-olds became a whole industry in NYC, tutoring companies charging thousands of dollars to coach preschoolers on pattern recognition and vocabulary tasks. The results were exactly what you'd predict: G&T programs were overwhelmingly white and Asian in a school system that's overwhelmingly Black and Latino. In some years something like 75% of offers went to white and Asian kids in a system where they're maybe 30% of enrollment. So you've got a publicly funded program that functions as a segregation engine, sorting kids by parental income at age four and then giving the sorted-in kids better resources, smaller class sizes, more experienced teachers for the next fourteen years.

De Blasio tried to kill the whole thing in 2021, replace it with an enrichment model available to more kids. Then Adams reversed that immediately when he took office because the G&T constituency is politically loud and organized, exactly those middle-class families the program was designed to retain. Mamdani's proposal to keep the 3rd grade entry but drop the kindergarten track is a compromise that at least acknowledges you can't meaningfully assess "giftedness" in someone who hasn't learned to read yet, while not blowing up the whole system and eating the political cost.

The deeper thing though is that the entire concept of "gifted" as a category you sort children into is doing ideological work. It naturalizes what are really class differences in early childhood environment. A four-year-old who knows more words has parents who read to them every night because they have the time and education to do so. The test amounts to discovering accumulated advantage, then labels it merit, then uses that label to justify giving already-advantaged kids more advantages. Outcome of structural inequality gets reframed as a natural market signal, and then policy responds to the signal rather than the structure. Basically capitalist logic.
