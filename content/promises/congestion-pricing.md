Mamdani was a consistent, unwavering supporter of this throughout his career. As mayor, he celebrated its one-year anniversary alongside Hochul and MTA Chair Lieber.

## What

It's a toll. You drive a car into Manhattan below 60th Street, you pay $9 during peak hours. That's it. Cameras on gantries read your license plate as you cross into the zone, you get charged automatically, same way E-ZPass works on a highway toll. Trucks pay more based on size. Off-peak hours are cheaper. There are some exemptions and credits for certain crossings.

The economic theory is straightforward: driving a car into the most congested part of the most densely populated city in the country imposes costs on everyone else. Pollution, noise, slower buses, pedestrian danger, emergency vehicle delays. Those are _externalities_ (costs that the driver doesn't pay but everyone else absorbs). Congestion pricing forces the driver to internalize some of those costs. The price signal changes behavior, some people stop driving and take transit instead, and the revenue funds the transit they switch to.

Same logic as a carbon tax or a cigarette tax. You're making the thing that harms others more expensive so that the price reflects the actual social cost rather than just the private cost to the individual.

This was the first program of its kind in the United States. London, Stockholm, and Singapore have had versions of this for years, but American car culture made it nearly impossible to implement here until New York finally did it.

## Backstory

In June 2024, right before congestion pricing was scheduled to launch, Hochul delayed it by about seven months, citing affordability concerns she said came from talking with people in diners. After the November 2024 election she pushed it forward again but at $9 instead of the planned $15.

Then Trump tried to kill it by revoking federal approval and withholding funding. A federal judge halted the Trump administration's effort and the MTA has been winning in court against nearly a dozen lawsuits.

## Results (Year One)

- 27 million fewer vehicles entered Manhattan south of 60th Street (11% reduction)
- $550 million in net revenue ($50 million more than projected)
- 22% drop in air pollution
- 17% fewer noise complaints to 311
- 4% increase in car speeds on weekdays
- 2.3% increase in bus speeds
- Subway/bus ridership up 7%
- LIRR ridership up 9%, Metro-North up 6%
- Lincoln Tunnel travel 25% faster, Holland Tunnel 51% faster
- 16 million more visits to the central business district than the previous year

The MTA is using the revenue to back $15 billion in bonds for transit improvements. As of January 2026, more than $6 billion in projects are already in construction: Second Avenue Subway Phase 2, ADA upgrades at nine stations, new signal systems serving 600,000+ A/C riders in Brooklyn and Queens.

None of the catastrophic predictions materialized. Manhattan businesses haven't fled, the city's economy hasn't contracted, the South Bronx isn't seeing more trucks and dirtier air. The toll revenues are also funding a new asthma case management program in the Bronx targeting neighborhoods like Mott Haven where heavy truck traffic historically caused high rates of respiratory illness.

One analyst who's been tracking it closely notes that the results, while genuinely positive, are "a bit less than advertised and not yet transformational." Monthly revenues are struggling to reach $50 million, below his projected $60 million, and the initial euphoria of freer-flowing streets has dissipated somewhat as traffic partially rebounded.

So yeah the program really was a success. But the $9 toll instead of $15 means less revenue and less behavioral impact than the program was designed for. Hochul's political cowardice in cutting the toll cost real money and real health outcomes.

## Macro

Congestion pricing is one of the few policies where the logic runs in the right direction. Instead of subsidizing private capital and hoping benefits trickle down to regular people, it directly prices a negative externality, forces those who impose costs on others to pay for them, and channels the revenue into public transit that disproportionately serves working-class riders.

It's still operating within market logic, but within that logic it's redistributive: car drivers who impose pollution, noise, danger, and congestion on everyone else are paying for transit improvements that benefit bus and subway riders. That's a transfer from a wealthier population (Manhattan car commuters) to a less wealthy one (transit-dependent New Yorkers).

And it's generating a stable, recurring revenue stream that doesn't depend on bond markets or federal grants, which, given [[promises/affordable-housing#money|everything we discussed about those being weaponized]], matters a lot.
