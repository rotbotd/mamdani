Mamdani's positions on Israel and Palestine were the most consistent of any policy area across his campaign — and the most politically dangerous. He is the most prominent elected official in the United States to call Israel's actions in Gaza a genocide, support BDS, and pledge to arrest Benjamin Netanyahu if he entered New York City.

## The Core Positions

Mamdani supported the Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions movement throughout his Assembly career and into the mayoral race. He characterized Israel's military operations in Gaza as genocide. He pledged to use the mayor's authority to arrest Netanyahu under the ICC warrant if he entered city jurisdiction. He stated Israel has "a right to exist" but declined to say it has a right to exist "as a Jewish state," saying he was "not comfortable supporting any state that has a hierarchy of citizenship on the basis of religion."

These positions were not politically convenient — they risked alienating Jewish voters, major donors, and the city's powerful pro-Israel institutional infrastructure. That he won anyway reflected both the changing demographics of the Democratic primary electorate and the depth of opposition to the Gaza war among younger voters.

## How the Messaging Evolved

While the substance remained constant, the framing softened noticeably over the course of the campaign.

His initial statement after October 7, 2023 mourned deaths "across Israel and Palestine" without naming Hamas. By mid-campaign, he explicitly called October 7 "a horrific war crime" and said Hamas should lay down its arms. He initially refused to condemn the phrase "globalize the intifada," saying "the role of the mayor is not to police language." He later told business leaders privately he would "discourage" the phrase, and said publicly at the debate that he understood it "evokes many painful memories" for Jewish New Yorkers.

On Cornell Tech and the Technion partnership on Roosevelt Island — which he had targeted for boycott since 2020, calling it an "Israeli institution involved in developing weapons for the IDF" — the Cornell Daily Sun reported he appeared to walk back prior calls to boycott after the election.

His 2023 statement that "when the boot of the NYPD is on your neck, it's been laced by the IDF" — a reference to NYPD-IDF training exchanges — was reframed during the general election as merely "a reference to training exercises."

## As Mayor

On Day 1, Mamdani revoked two Adams-era executive orders: one restricting city employees from participating in Israel boycotts and another adopting the International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance definition of antisemitism (which critics argued conflated criticism of Israel with antisemitism). He kept the Office to Combat Antisemitism and appointed a director from the progressive New York Jewish Agenda, signaling that combating antisemitism and criticizing Israeli policy are not contradictory.

He reportedly used the word "genocide" standing next to President Trump in the Oval Office during their meeting — a moment that, if confirmed, would be extraordinary given Trump's enthusiastic support for Israel's military operations.

## The Political Landscape

The Israel/Palestine issue cut across Mamdani's coalition in unusual ways. His strongest pro-Palestinian positions energized younger voters, Arab and Muslim communities, and the progressive activist base. They cost him support among older Jewish voters and institutional donors. The general election opponent tried to make his positions disqualifying; it didn't work.

As mayor, the issue is less central to daily governance than housing or transit, but it remains a symbolic flashpoint — particularly given the anti-Muslim hostility directed at him (the Gracie Mansion bombing, Tuberville's posts, Sid Rosenberg's slurs). His willingness to maintain these positions under literal physical threat has made him a national figure in the Palestinian solidarity movement.

The question going forward is whether BDS commitments translate into concrete municipal action — reviewing city contracts, pension fund investments, procurement policies — or remain primarily rhetorical. The city's institutional ties to Israel are deep (NYPD training partnerships, university research collaborations, financial sector connections) and unwinding them would generate enormous resistance.
