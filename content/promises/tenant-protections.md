Revive the Mayor's Office to Protect Tenants, crack down on negligent landlords, and intervene when buildings fall into disrepair.

## What

So the Mayor's Office to Protect Tenants (MOPT) is a coordinating body that's supposed to defend tenants' rights, pressure landlords, and make sure city agencies act quickly when renters face unsafe or illegal conditions. It existed before but had been weakened. Mamdani revitalized it on Day 1.

Here's how it actually works: right now at least five different agencies, HPD, DOB, FDNY, DEP, DOH, are responsible for different housing maintenance issues. So when a tenant reports a problem, they get bounced between agencies and nothing happens. MOPT is supposed to coordinate all of that under one roof, from inspectors to the lawyers who litigate in Housing Court. The executive order mandates that all mayoral agencies cooperate with MOPT, which on paper gives it real coordinating authority.

Then there's Executive Order 08, the "Rental Ripoff" hearings. MOPT along with HPD, DOB, and DCWP are required to hold at least one hearing in each borough where tenants can testify about conditions, hidden fees, and landlord abuses. Within ninety days of the last hearing, those agencies have to submit a joint report proposing a plan to address harmful landlord practices through existing enforcement powers, improved code enforcement, consumer protection powers, and policy changes, with a specific mandate that violations get logged and corrected on a "significantly faster timeline."

Now, under existing but underutilized law, the city already has the power to complete repairs itself and bill the landlord. This is called Emergency Repair Program (ERP) - HPD can hire contractors to fix hazardous conditions and then place a lien on the property for the cost. The city has had this power for decades but rarely uses it aggressively because it requires upfront spending and political will to go after landlords. Mamdani's platform explicitly says he'll use that power, and he's pledged to double the Alternative Enforcement Program (AEP) and expand the Office of Special Enforcement so repeat offenders face higher fines.

The AEP is worth understanding: it targets the worst buildings in the city, the ones with the most violations, and subjects them to intensive inspections and expedited repair timelines. If landlords don't comply, the city can appoint an administrator to take over building management. Doubling this program means going after twice as many bad buildings. The Office of Special Enforcement handles the really egregious stuff - illegal conversions, tenant harassment, fraud. Expanding it means more investigators and lawyers to build cases.

For the history: MOPT was originally created in 2019 under de Blasio through Executive Order 39. Then under Mayor Adams it was defunded and deprioritized. That's the "weakened" part. Adams also cut $25 million from the Anti-Harassment Tenant Protection program over three years, and fully cut funding for Neighborhood Pillars, a program that let the city work with tenant associations to purchase and renovate distressed buildings. So it wasn't just neglect, it was active dismantling of the infrastructure that existed to hold landlords accountable.

## Since Taking Office

Day 1: Executive Order 03 revitalized MOPT and appointed Cea Weaver as director. Weaver is a longtime housing organizer who helped lead the campaign for the 2019 rent law reforms. The appointment signals this isn't just bureaucratic reshuffling - they're putting movement people in charge.

Mid-January: MOPT staff started going door-to-door in buildings with chronic violations, informing tenants of their rights and documenting conditions. This is the "proactive enforcement" approach Mamdani campaigned on - not waiting for complaints but going out and finding problems.

January 16: The administration announced a $2.1 million settlement against a landlord for tenant harassment and unsafe conditions. Mamdani held the press conference in the lobby of the building itself, with affected tenants present. The staging matters: it's a signal to other bad landlords that the city is watching and will make examples.

The Rental Ripoff hearings are underway. Brooklyn (Downtown Brooklyn, February 26) and Queens (Long Island City, March 5) already happened. Remaining hearings: Bronx (Fordham, March 11), Manhattan (East Harlem, March 28), and Staten Island (North Shore, April 7). Each session has MOPT, HPD, DOB, and DCWP officials present to hear testimony directly from tenants. There's also a resource fair at each hearing where tenants can get help with individual issues on the spot. The policy report is due 90 days after the final hearing, so expect concrete enforcement proposals by early July.

## Why It Matters

The basic question is: who does the city work for? Under Adams, enforcement was slow, understaffed, and often favored landlords who knew how to game the system. Tenants with dangerous conditions waited months for inspections while landlords faced minimal consequences for ignoring violations. The asymmetry is structural - landlords have lawyers and property managers, tenants have jobs and lives and can't spend all day navigating city bureaucracy.

Mamdani's approach treats housing as infrastructure, not just a market. The city already has the legal tools to force repairs, take over mismanaged buildings, and fine bad actors into compliance. What was missing was political will. MOPT under Weaver is an attempt to change that - to make the existing powers actually work for tenants instead of sitting unused.

The risk is that this stays symbolic. Press conferences and hearings are good optics but don't fix anyone's heat. The test will be the enforcement numbers six months from now: how many ERP repairs, how many AEP buildings, how many landlords actually paying fines. If those numbers don't move significantly, the promises were empty.
