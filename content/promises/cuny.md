Make CUNY and SUNY tuition-free, funded by taxes on high earners. As an assemblyman, Mamdani introduced the REPAIR Act to eliminate Columbia and NYU's property tax exemptions (~$321 million/year) and redirect the revenue to CUNY.

## The REPAIR Act

REPAIR (Repeal Egregious Property Accumulation and Invest It Right) is **state legislation**, not city. It would remove property tax exemptions for private universities that had more than $100 million written off their taxes in the prior fiscal year. In practice, that means only Columbia and NYU get hit.

The tax exemption is enshrined in the state constitution, which means Albany lawmakers would have to approve the measure in _two consecutive legislative sessions_, after which it goes directly to voters at the ballot box. That's a constitutional amendment process, not a regular bill, so even with legislative support, you're looking at a multi-year timeline before any money comes out of it.

As of late 2024, the bill had four cosponsors in the Senate and 12 in the Assembly, with John Liu carrying it in the Senate. Mamdani and others reintroduced the bill in January after it died in committee. It hasn't moved through yet. The revenue at stake is about $321-327 million annually, all directed to CUNY.

## What Can the Mayor Actually Do?

As mayor, Mamdani cannot pass REPAIR. That's Albany.

But the city spends approximately $600 million annually for CUNY community colleges, while the state handles around $2.5 billion annually for the senior colleges. So the mayor has direct budgetary authority over the community college funding stream. Adams was slashing that consistently. Mamdani **can reverse those cuts and increase city-side CUNY funding** without asking Albany for anything.

Beyond direct funding: the bully pulpit and movement infrastructure. PSC president James Davis explicitly framed the post-election strategy as converting the 100,000 campaign volunteers into a pressure campaign demanding Albany meet the needs of ordinary New Yorkers, with CUNY funding and ending tuition as central demands. Let's really hope he goes through on this! 🤞🤞

## The Structural Picture

CUNY's crisis is the same story as [[promises/nycha|NYCHA]], as the MTA, as every public institution in the city.

Only 8% of CUNY buildings are in good repair. Deferred maintenance backlog: $5 billion. Over 80% of CUNY undergraduates are students of color. 50% come from families earning less than $30,000 a year. The system propels vastly more low-income students into the middle class than the entire Ivy League combined.

Meanwhile:

Columbia bought a 2.2-acre property near the West Harlem waterfront for $84 million in cash and _won't pay property taxes on it_ once converted to educational use. Estimated savings of $12 million over the following decade on that single purchase.

Columbia is the largest private landowner in New York City with properties valued close to $4 billion. They use the tax exemption not just for classrooms but as a competitive real estate advantage, meaning they can outbid everyone else when land comes up for sale because their carrying costs will be zero.

So you have two institutions, Columbia and NYU, with combined endowments of $20 billion, paying nothing in property taxes on billions in real estate, using the exemption to actively expand their land empires, while the public university system that actually educates working-class New Yorkers has buildings literally leaking brown liquid onto students. Hell no.

Literally: the money Columbia and NYU don't pay is the money CUNY doesn't have.

## Problem

Columbia and NYU are _massive employers_, massive donors to political campaigns, and deeply embedded in the city's power structure. They'll fight this hard. Mamdani needs to convert the city's populace into an organized movement to pressure Albany.
