Mamdani's labor platform centers on a theory of change rarely seen from elected officials: "to take on inequality, we know that the most effective tool is union density." Not platitudes about unions building the middle class — the actual mechanics of worker power.

## $30 by '30 Minimum Wage

The signature proposal: raise NYC's minimum wage from $16.50 to $30 by 2030. The timeline is $20 in 2027, $23.50 in 2028, $27 in 2029, $30 in 2030, with automatic cost-of-living or productivity adjustments thereafter.

This requires Albany — the city can't set its own wage floor without state authorization. So like many Mamdani proposals, the mayor's role is advocacy and pressure, not direct implementation.

## Deputy Mayor for Economic Justice

Mamdani created a new Deputy Mayor for Economic Justice position overseeing TLC, Consumer and Worker Protection, Human Rights Commission, and Small Business Services. He appointed former acting U.S. Labor Secretary Julie Su — a significant signal about labor priorities at the cabinet level.

He also pledged to reshape the TLC from a punitive enforcement body into a driver-advocacy agency, with a driver retirement plan and living wage standard for TLC-licensed drivers. Expanded protections for delivery and gig workers. Supported worker-owned cooperatives and public banking (he co-sponsored the New York Public Banking Act as an assemblyman).

## Union Density as Strategy

The New Labor Forum piece by Eric Blanc and Brandon Mancilla (UAW Region 9A director) lays out the theory: in the most union-dense city in the country, Mamdani's affordability platform needs the institutional support of unions and the 700,000+ workers they represent. The labor movement needs a mayor who understands that organizing is the path, not just endorsements.

Most city unions endorsed Cuomo in the primary or declined to endorse. Only a handful backed Mamdani going in — UAW stood out as the only major international union, with Region 9A as the first to endorse. After the primary win, endorsements cascaded: Hotel & Gaming Trades Council, 32BJ SEIU, 1199SEIU, NYSNA, UFT, CWA, the Central Labor Council.

The campaign itself operated like an organizing drive: 100,000 volunteers, thousands of developed leaders, multiracial and multigenerational. The question is whether that energy can move from electoral campaign to workplace organizing.

## What the Mayor Can Actually Do

Federal labor law limits city-level action, but there's more room than people think:

**Bully pulpit.** By far the most impactful thing Mamdani could do requires no legislation and costs nothing. Use his massive platform to encourage New Yorkers to unionize, galvanize public support for campaigns, and call out union-busting. Host online unionization trainings. Make viral videos about labor struggles. A "100,000 New Union Jobs" goal as a four-year target.

**Labor Peace Agreements.** City law since 2021 allows workers at contracted human services and some economic development projects to demand employers not interfere with unionization. This covers about 80,000 human services workers — disproportionately non-white and female, average childcare worker income only $25,000 in 2023. Mamdani can strengthen this through executive order, expanding LPA requirements to all workers at establishments receiving city funding. That's potentially huge given 18% of NYC workers are employed by non-profits.

**Doubling DCWP.** Mamdani pledged to double the Department of Consumer and Worker Protection from ~450 employees to 1,000. This costs the city almost nothing — DCWP pays for itself through fines and licenses. Last fiscal year it brought in $21.5 million while spending only $17.5 million on staffing.

**Secure Jobs Act.** Support for "just cause" job protections for all NYC workers, introduced by Council Member Tiffany Cabán. Research shows employers break labor laws in 41.5% of union drives, often through illegal firings. Once workers understand they can't get fired for organizing, we can expect more campaigns.

## Protected Time Off (Local Law 145)

Effective February 22, 2026: employers must make "reasonable effort" to find coverage before calling sick employees in to work. Applies to all NYC employers regardless of size. DCWP enforces with fines starting at $500 per violation.

The enforcement approach is clever: using CDC health data to flag employers with suspiciously low sick leave usage patterns.

"When life happens, your job shouldn't be the thing that falls apart."

## The UAW Model

UAW Region 9A has been on an organizing tear for a decade, concentrated in higher education and medical research, museums and cultural institutions, non-profits and legal services, and auto dealerships. Since the mid-2010s, they've won recognition for over 10,000 academic workers across Columbia, NYU, the New School, Mt. Sinai, Weill Cornell, Rockefeller, Einstein, School of Visual Arts, and NY Film Academy.

Local 2325 has organized over 2,000 workers at 43 nonprofits since 2017 — now representing attorneys and staff at nearly all the city's public defender agencies. Their density allowed coordinated bargaining around a common expiration date and a sectoral strike strategy.

Local 2110 has launched massive organizing at museums: MoMA, Guggenheim, Whitney, New Museum, Tenement Museum, Bronx Museum, Brooklyn Museum, Jewish Museum, Dia Art Foundation, and more. Met workers went public with their drive last summer.

The question is whether this can scale across the labor movement, with Mamdani as catalyst.

## The Context

Private sector unionization in NYC is only 13.7%, almost half of what it was in the 1980s. Public sector is 65.5% unionized, but overall only one in five NYC workers belongs to a union — down from one in three in the 1970s.

Trump has kneecapped the NLRB, making federal organizing harder. Most unions remain focused on servicing shrinking membership rather than growth. But there's unprecedented public support for unions, and the Mamdani volunteer army represents thousands of potential workplace organizers.

The comparison point is Mayor La Guardia in the 1930s, who leaned on and boosted organized labor to lift the city out of the Depression. La Guardia pledged to make New York a "100 percent union city": "if the right to live interferes with profits, profits must necessarily give way to that right."
