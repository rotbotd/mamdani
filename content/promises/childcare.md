Free childcare for all NYC children ages 6 weeks to 5 years old, expanding pre-K and 3-K. Estimated cost: $5–6 billion annually. Pay parity for childcare workers with public school teachers.

A 2025 Comptroller report found the average cost of childcare for infants and toddlers was about $18,200 a year for family-based options (up 79% since 2019) and $26,000 for center-based care (up 43%).

For a working-class family paying rent-stabilized housing and commuting by bus, childcare can easily be the single largest expense.

## What

Eight days into his administration, Mamdani stood next to Hochul and announced a $1.7 billion childcare deal.

Hochul committed $1.7 billion in new childcare investment for the upcoming fiscal year, bringing total statewide investment to $4.5 billion for childcare and pre-K services. The breakdown: $1.2 billion goes toward increasing subsidies for low-cost childcare assistance programs, a dramatic increase from last year. The rest funds new programs.

The headline program is called **2Care**: free childcare for two-year-olds in NYC. It starts small with about 2,000 children this fall for $75 million, then expands to $425 million in the second year, adding 10,000 more seats. It continues expanding for two more years until fully phased in to serve an estimated 55,000 two-year-olds. Mamdani said it'll be fully universal by the end of his first term.

About $100 million also goes toward fixing the existing 3-K program to ensure the city actually has seats in neighborhoods where demand hasn't been met. One of the absurd problems under Adams was that parents in Flatbush were being offered seats in Astoria.

Additionally, all four-year-old children in the entire state will have pre-K access by the 2028-2029 school year. This sounds like it should already exist since New York has supposedly had "universal pre-K" for a decade, but as Hochul admitted, _many communities never actually implemented it_.

The announcement came right as the Trump administration attempted to freeze billions in federal childcare funding for New York. Jesus Christ.

Hochul said the administration told the state two days before the announcement that they'd claw back $3.6 billion and demanded Social Security numbers of all beneficiaries within two weeks, which she called a "fishing expedition" she'd fight in court.

## Workforce

You can't create 55,000 new childcare seats if there's nobody to staff them, so the state is creating a new "Office of Child Care and Early Education", investing in scholarships for early childhood educators, and expanding SUNY and CUNY degree programs to train early childhood teachers.

Mamdani appointed Emmy Liss (de Blasio-era pre-K architect) to run a new city Office of Child Care.

His preliminary FY27 budget includes $2.2 billion for Universal Pre-K and $582.6 million for Early Childhood Education Investment.

## Why did Hochul do this?

The DSA cochair said "there's no doubt in my mind that this is entirely due to the Zohran campaign and the movement behind it."

That's probably right. Hochul is a moderate up for reelection. She didn't wake up one morning and decide to spend $1.7 billion on childcare. She's standing next to a democratic socialist mayor because the political ground shifted underneath her.

## Here to stay?

Hochul sidestepped questions on whether the state will cover costs after the second year and whether new taxes are on the table.

As Jacobin pointed out, remembering the expiration of Biden's child tax credit and the political backlash that followed, two-year funding commitments for programs like this are worrisome. Social benefits once given should not be taken away. If Hochul loses reelection or decides the politics have shifted, the state funding could evaporate and the city would be stuck either funding it alone or cutting the program after families have come to depend on it.

## Baby Baskets

A smaller proposal: Finnish-style "baby baskets" for every new family in NYC (~125,000 annually). Packages of diapers, nursing supplies, books, baby care items, and maternal health guides. Estimated cost: under $20 million/year. Announced on WNYC's Brian Lehrer Show in January 2025.

Finland has done this since 1938. The boxes are famous and they even double as the baby's first bed.
