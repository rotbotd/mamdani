The most distinctive proposal: city-operated grocery stores. A public option for produce.

## City-Owned Grocery Stores

One per borough as a pilot. The stores would use city land, buy at wholesale prices, receive discounted utilities, and be exempt from property taxes. Targeted at neighborhoods where 30%+ of residents are food insecure.

Estimated cost: $60 million/year.

This hasn't moved yet — it requires startup capital and operational planning that hasn't appeared in budget documents. The preliminary budget raised baseline food insecurity program funding to $53.6 million, but the grocery stores themselves remain aspirational.

## The Logic

Food deserts in NYC are real. Large swaths of the South Bronx, Central Brooklyn, and Southeast Queens have no full-service grocery stores within walking distance. Residents rely on bodegas, dollar stores, and fast food — or travel long distances for groceries.

Private grocery chains avoid these neighborhoods because margins are thin and theft rates are higher. The result is a market failure: the people who most need access to fresh food can't get it, because serving them isn't profitable.

The public option theory: if the city operates stores without a profit motive, with subsidized land and utilities, it can serve neighborhoods that private chains won't. The stores become public infrastructure, like libraries or fire stations, rather than businesses that need to turn a profit.

## Status

Not yet addressed. No public action or announcement in the first two months. This likely requires the Albany tax revenue that hasn't materialized — $60 million/year is real money when you're staring at a $5.4 billion deficit.
