The most distinctive proposal: city-operated grocery stores. A public option for produce.

## City-Owned Grocery Stores

One per borough as a pilot. The stores would use city land, buy at wholesale prices, receive discounted utilities, and be exempt from property taxes. Targeted at neighborhoods where 30%+ of residents are food insecure.

Estimated cost: $60 million to launch all five stores, redirecting the $140 million the city currently spends on tax breaks and incentives for private grocery stores. Mamdani based his estimates on a Chicago feasibility study, adjusted for NYC's higher costs.

This hasn't moved yet — it requires startup capital and operational planning that hasn't appeared in budget documents. The preliminary budget raised baseline food insecurity program funding to $53.6 million, but the grocery stores themselves remain aspirational.

## The Problem: Food Deserts Are Real

According to USDA data, 53.6 million Americans — 17.4% of the population — live in low-income areas with low access to grocery stores (defined as half a mile in urban areas, 10 miles in rural). At least 3 million New York City residents live in neighborhoods without easy access to fresh food.

Large swaths of the South Bronx, Central Brooklyn, and Southeast Queens have no full-service grocery stores within walking distance. In one of Mamdani's Queens districts, constituents report multiple fast food restaurants but no grocery stores at all. Residents rely on bodegas, dollar stores, and fast food — or travel long distances for groceries.

The health consequences are documented: low-income residents of food deserts are significantly more likely to suffer from diet-related diseases like diabetes and cardiovascular disease. This isn't just inconvenience — it's a public health crisis shaped by where capital chooses to invest.

## Why Markets Fail Here

Private grocery chains avoid these neighborhoods because margins are thin and perceived risk is higher. Supermarkets typically operate on 1-3% margins; smaller stores need 3-5% or more, and it takes years to reach even that level. The result is a textbook market failure: the people who most need access to fresh food can't get it, because serving them isn't profitable enough.

Corporate consolidation makes this worse. A significant portion of all grocery sales go to just four brands: Walmart, Kroger, Costco, and Albertsons. These giants have spent decades using market dominance to extract deals from suppliers that smaller stores can't command. The Robinson-Patman Act was supposed to prevent this — it made it illegal for suppliers to give preferential pricing to large retailers without offering the same deals to everyone. It was enforced vigorously until the 1980s, then abandoned in Reagan's deregulatory push. Independent grocers couldn't compete, and low-income communities lost their stores.

Chicago's experience is instructive: the city spent $10 million on tax breaks to lure a Whole Foods to the underserved South Side under Mayor Rahm Emanuel. It closed after just six years. The subsidy model doesn't work — you're paying private capital to do something it doesn't want to do, and it leaves the moment the incentives dry up.

## The Public Option Theory

If the city operates stores without a profit motive, with subsidized land and utilities, it can serve neighborhoods that private chains won't. The stores become public infrastructure, like libraries or fire stations, rather than businesses that need to turn a profit.

The advantages of public ownership:

Property tax exemption alone can increase margins by 1-3% — often the difference between a store staying open and closing. A government can be patient, absorbing losses in the first 3-4 years while a store builds its customer base. There's no incentive to jack up prices when inflation provides cover; a public store can demonstrate what prices would actually be without profit extraction. The stores anchor future investment, establishing foot traffic patterns that de-risk later private investment.

The challenge is procurement. Can a city-owned store buy food from suppliers at prices competitive with Walmart? Mamdani's plan relies on wholesale purchasing and centralized warehousing. Even if they can't match big-box prices exactly, the rent and tax savings should compensate.

This isn't untested. Erie, Kansas has owned Stub's Market since 2020, buying it when the previous owner was about to close and leave the town without any grocery store. St. Paul, Kansas runs a municipal grocery that actually turns a small profit, reinvested into operations. Madison, Wisconsin is opening a city-owned store this year. Atlanta has two publicly-run grocery stores in development. Venice, Illinois secured state funding for a public-private model where the city owns the infrastructure and leases to a private operator.

## The Political Stakes

"This is an opportunity to show what the price could actually be if we were being honest about supply chain issues, honest about the impact of profits as part of this equation," Mamdani said during the campaign.

The stores aren't meant to be a safety net just for the poorest New Yorkers. Mamdani framed them as "the most obvious choice for New Yorkers of each and all class backgrounds" — if the pilot succeeds, the plan is to build more until every New Yorker can afford the groceries they need.

Critics call this "textbook socialism." They're right, and that's the point. When markets systematically fail to provide a basic necessity, the question becomes whether you keep subsidizing private capital to pretend it's interested, or whether you build public capacity to do what capital won't.

Chicago's recent pivot is worth noting: they planned to be the first major city with a publicly-owned grocery store, but couldn't find an experienced operator to qualify for state funding. They're now opening a public market instead — still publicly owned, but housing local retailers and farmers rather than operating as a full grocery store. It's a compromise, but it's still public infrastructure filling a gap that private capital abandoned.

## Status

Not yet addressed. No public action or announcement in the first two months. This likely requires fiscal room that doesn't exist yet — $60 million is real money when you're staring at a $5.4 billion deficit, even if it's ultimately redirected from existing grocery subsidies.

The political will is there. The budget isn't. Yet.
