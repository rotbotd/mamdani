Build bus lanes, bike lanes, and car-free streets. Unlike most transit policy, this is something the mayor can actually do.

## Why

The basic argument is that streets are public land, and right now the vast majority of that public land is allocated to the private automobile. In a city where most people don't own cars. New York City is a place where the majority of residents get around by transit, walking, and biking, but the streets are designed as if everyone drives. That's a massive public advantage to car owners, who on average are wealthier than transit riders, at the expense of everyone else.

When a bus is stuck in traffic going four miles per hour on Fordham Road, that's because the street has been designed to prioritize cars, _including illegally parked ones_, over the 130,000 people who ride that bus every day. Those bus riders are disproportionately working class, disproportionately Black and Latino, disproportionately people who don't have the option of driving. Their time is being stolen every single day by a street design that treats their movement as less important than car storage. So reallocating road space from cars to buses is a direct material intervention that puts time back into the lives of working people. Time that's currently being extracted from them by a political choice about how public space gets used.

Same logic for bike lanes. A protected bike lane is the _cheapest_ transportation infrastructure you can build per person moved. It takes almost no public subsidy to operate, unlike roads which require constant maintenance for heavy vehicles. It produces zero emissions, zero noise, zero pedestrian danger relative to cars. And the people who are getting killed and maimed on NYC streets are overwhelmingly pedestrians and cyclists, not people inside cars. So when someone dies on "Crashland" Ashland Place because the city refused to build a protected lane after a developer made a phone call, that's also an implicit choice that valued a real estate firm's convenience over human life.

So, basically, who does public infrastructure serve? The road in front of your house is public land. The question is whether it's organized to move the most people safely and efficiently, which means buses, bikes, and pedestrians, or whether it's organized to store and move private vehicles for the minority who own them. Every bus lane is public space being reclaimed for public use.

## Why This Is Different

The mayor's power over streets is one of the few areas where he has nearly **unilateral authority**. The city controls its own streets, parking enforcement, bike and bus lanes. Former NYC traffic commissioner Sam Schwartz said Mamdani can push through significant changes "almost" on his own. He doesn't need Albany. He doesn't need the MTA. The DOT is a city agency with about 6,000 employees and a billion dollar budget that reports directly to the mayor.

So unlike [[promises/free-buses|free buses]], which require the governor and MTA to cooperate, street redesign is where the mayor's office can just act.

## Mayor Adams

The City Council passed a Streets Master Plan law in 2019 requiring 150 new miles of bus lanes and 250 miles of new bike lanes over five years. Adams _consistently failed_ to meet those requirements because his administration imposed a hiring freeze that caused DOT to lose dozens of employees in key bus-planning units between 2019 and 2024. He actively starved the agency of capacity. Transportation Alternatives put it bluntly: "Mayor Eric Adams governed like he had never heard of them."

Worse than neglect, Adams killed specific projects because of political connections. The Ashland Place bike lane was killed after Two Trees executives lobbied Adams' advisor Ingrid Lewis-Martin, who was later indicted for bribery. The Fordham Road bus lanes were blocked after lobbying from a US Representative and a City Council member.

Mamdani put it: "For too long, our transit decisions of this city have been made off of well-placed phone calls rather than the needs of working people."

## What Mamdani Is Doing

Appointed transit advocate Mike Flynn as DOT commissioner — literally his first act after taking the oath.

Revived four halted bus/bike lane projects killed by Adams:

- Offset bus lanes on Fordham Road (serving 130,000 daily riders)
- Protected bike lane on "Crashland" Ashland Place in Brooklyn
- Bike networks in East Flatbush and Ditmas Park

Completed the full safety redesign of McGuinness Boulevard in Greenpoint on Day 3.

Pledged to create car-free streets in front of every school (universal, not opt-in).

Co-introduced a weight-based vehicle registration fee to discourage heavier SUVs and trucks.

His preliminary budget adds $5 million per year for bus and bike lane projects over four years and 20 new DOT positions specifically devoted to these projects by mid-2027.

## Opposition

Some Fordham Road business owners oppose the bus lanes, arguing they'll hurt business, same argument that killed them under Adams. I'm not sure there's actual substance to this, so let's get into the interesting opposition dynamic:

## FDNY Opposition

The FDNY (Fire Department of New York) opposes protected bike lanes. Just days ago, a top FDNY official testified before the City Council that the Fire Department claims they obstruct emergency access. This contradicts the administration's position and the evidence from cities like Paris where bike infrastructure actually _reduced_ emergency response times.

This is a really instructive case study in how institutional resistance to progressive policy actually works in practice, so let's go over it:

The FDNY argument is this: when you install a protected bike lane with physical barriers between the bike lane and the car lane, fire trucks can't pull up as close to the curb as they normally would. Chief Kevin Woods testified that they need more than the standard 30 feet of clearance around hydrants because "you're coming from a distance away from that curb now, you have to make a sharp turn into that hydrant, which means we could be blocking the street as well."

The firefighters union president piled on, saying "none of these changes should be made until there is a full review by a trained department unit in coordination with the DOT."

Sounds reasonable on the surface, right? Nobody wants fire trucks blocked. But there's frankly a kind of hilarious dimension to this.

First, the DOT official sitting right next to Woods at the hearing seemed "perplexed" by his testimony and said "this is the first time we're hearing about the additional feet needed beyond the 30 feet."

The FDNY hadn't raised this issue through normal inter-agency channels. They brought it to a City Council hearing chaired by a conservative car-first Council member, Joann Ariola, who used it as a platform to attack bike infrastructure. After Streetsblog reached out to City Hall during the hearing, Woods actually backtracked, saying "Just for the record, I mentioned previously the FDNY was against protected bike lanes. We're not against protected bike lanes."

So the chief of fire operations publicly contradicted the mayor's agenda, then walked it back when the press noticed.

The "emergency access" argument has also been deliberately weaponized as a procedural tool to block bike lanes. A 2009 law requires DOT to consult with FDNY and other agencies before any street redesign longer than three blocks. Ariola added another regulation forcing DOT to get not just headquarters sign-off but written confirmation from every affected firehouse. So now to install a four-block bike lane using a standard design DOT has deployed for years, they need sign-offs from four separate siloed agencies plus individual firehouses. As the former DOT policy director who fought the original law said, "it was really just an obstructionist move to put a spanner in the works."

Secondly, this is not really new. It's a pattern going back years. Back in 2019, the FDNY itself said that traffic, not bike lanes, is to blame for increased response times. Car ownership in NYC was up 9%, ride-hail trips had increased 62%, and the former president of the firefighters union specifically warned that the city's constant gridlock from cars posed a real risk to emergency response. The thing slowing down fire trucks is cars. Protected bike lanes actually reduce overall traffic and have been proven to make streets safer for everyone.

Adams' own fire commissioner Laura Kavanagh, after leaving the job, explicitly criticized this dynamic, saying that citing emergency services every time opponents want to stop a bike lane "diluted our ability to raise a red flag when there is one." She said people should see the two agencies as "partners in the fight to make streets safer, rather than as adversaries."

So what's actually going on? Fire departments are conservative institutions with deep institutional cultures organized around vehicle access. Their members overwhelmingly drive to work, think from a "windshield perspective," and have an organizational interest in maintaining maximum road access for their apparatus. That's understandable. But the claim that protected bike lanes endanger public safety gets the causation exactly backwards. Cars kill over a hundred people a year on NYC streets. Protected bike lanes reduce deaths and serious injuries for all road users, including drivers and pedestrians, by double-digit percentages.
