$3.3 billion plan to retrofit 500 school buildings with rooftop solar and upgraded HVAC, build 500 green schoolyards, convert 50 schools into year-round resilience hubs, create 15,000 union jobs, and save ~$275 million/year in energy costs.

## Why Schools?

The volunteer group of climate experts who helped develop the plan (who asked to remain anonymous because of fear of retribution from the Trump administration) came to schools as the entry point because NYC's existing climate policies have failed the question of the affordability crisis and have failed the question of public goods.

They identified the "Green New Deal for Schools" concept as a kind of unicorn of climate policy: very popular in polling, has the support of labor because it involves unionized work, and people like their neighborhood schools even though they've been underfunded over three decades of austerity.

Instead of doing climate policy as a separate technocratic domain where you set emissions targets and timelines that nobody can feel in their daily life, you embed climate action inside the institution that 900,000 students and their families interact with every single day. The school building is simultaneously the climate intervention site, the jobs program site, the environmental justice site, and the public goods restoration site.

## The Three Prongs

**1. Retrofit 500 schools** with rooftop solar and HVAC upgrades. Currently only 119 of the city's 1,800 public schools have rooftop solar panels. The physical condition of these buildings is genuinely terrible, you got kids sitting in hallways with lead paint, asbestos, crumbling windows and roofs, classrooms with broken ventilation, no air conditioning in buildings that hit 90+ degrees, heating systems still burning No. 4 oil which is one of the most polluting fuels around. You literally HAVE to upgrade to HVAC if you care about student health. There's zero way around this.

**2. Build 500 green schoolyards**, transforming heat-absorbing asphalt into green space. During a heat dome event, the difference between an asphalt schoolyard and a green one is multiple degrees of surface temperature. During heavy rain, the difference is whether stormwater runs off into the combined sewer system and backs up into basements or gets absorbed on site. You're taking acres of asphalt in the densest neighborhoods and converting them to permeable green space.

**3. Transform 50 schools into resilience hubs**, modeled on hubs first built in Baltimore that are now a feature of climate adaptation plans in 250+ cities. Local schools residents already know and trust become the go-to location during heat waves, flooding, power outages. The policy ties these to battery storage, with Mamdani arguing that prioritizing resilience hubs with battery storage would help move forward closures of fossil fuel plants near schools (power plants are disproportionately sited in communities of color).

## Numbers

- Transitioning NYC public schools to renewable energy could reduce city emissions by 713,382 metric tons of CO2 equivalent per year, eliminating 1.5% of all citywide emissions
- ~$275 million annually in energy savings
- 15,000 union jobs

Two guiding principles: **use of union labor**, and **combating environmental racism** by prioritizing out-of-date school facilities most often located in communities of color. The union labor requirement builds a coalition between organized labor and climate advocates that has historically been fractured. The environmental racism prioritization means the worst buildings in the poorest neighborhoods get fixed first, not last.

## Context

Adams had already started something in this direction: a plan to retrofit 100 school buildings to no longer burn fossil fuels for heating, estimated at $4 billion by 2030, with $2 billion already committed. Mamdani is scaling that dramatically (100 → 500) while adding the schoolyards and resilience hub components Adams' plan didn't include.

Starting January 1, 2026, New York State requires that most newly constructed buildings be fully powered by electricity under the All-Electric Buildings Act, so new school construction will already be fossil-fuel-free.

## The "Why spend on solar when kids can't read" Argument

The criticism from the right is basically Heartland Institute stuff being recycled: why spend $3.3 billion on solar panels when kids can't read?

It's also a false choice. The buildings are physically deteriorating regardless. The HVAC systems **need replacement regardless** - we talked about this above. The question is whether you replace them with more fossil fuel infrastructure or with clean energy infrastructure. The $275 million in annual energy _savings_ literally goes back into the education budget.
