Goal: Double the city's annual capital investment in public housing from ~$1.27 billion, addressing an $80 billion repair backlog. Push Albany for matching state investment.

## What

NYCHA is the largest public housing authority in North America, with about 177,000 apartments housing over 360,000 people. Decades of federal disinvestment left an $80 billion backlog of repairs: broken elevators, mold, lead paint, failing heat systems. The buildings are falling apart faster than the city can fix them.

Mamdani wants to double the city's annual capital investment. He'd also build new affordable housing on underutilized NYCHA land (parking lots, storage areas, empty parcels), keeping it within the public system rather than selling to private developers.

## Clean Heat for All

On February 4, 2026, Mamdani announced a $38.4 million investment to install heat pumps at Beach 41st Street Houses in Edgemere, Queens. This is part of the Clean Heat for All initiative, a state-city collaboration that started in 2021 under the previous administration.

The investment delivers modern heating and cooling to 712 homes. The heat pumps replace an aging gas boiler system that had exceeded its useful life. Residents get individual temperature controls, more consistent heating, efficient cooling in bedrooms and living rooms, and better hot water reliability.

The program targets 10,000 NYCHA apartments by 2030. Early results from a pilot at Woodside Houses showed 86% decrease in energy required for space heating and 50% decrease in heating costs. 89% of residents with heat pumps reported satisfaction.

This is interesting because it addresses multiple problems at once: infrastructure decay, climate emissions, and quality of life. Mamdani framed it explicitly: "This is what it looks like when we understand that the fight for a livable planet and livable housing are in fact one and the same."

The investment also signals that the administration is willing to spend real money on NYCHA, not just talk about it. $38.4 million for one development, with plans to scale.

## PACT/RAD

There's an existing program called PACT (Permanent Affordability Commitment Together), part of a federal initiative from HUD (Department of Housing and Urban Development, the federal agency that oversees public housing nationwide).

The way it works: public housing gets funding through "Section 9" of the Housing Act, which gives money directly to housing authorities like NYCHA. But Section 9 funding has been gutted over decades.

Enter: "Section 8", which is a different funding stream. Vouchers that subsidize rent at privately-managed buildings. Section 8 money is more reliable and lets buildings access private financing for repairs.

PACT converts NYCHA buildings from Section 9 to Section 8, then leases them to private management companies. The private managers get access to financing that NYCHA can't, do the repairs, and run the buildings going forward.

That said, PACT is pretty much the exact mechanism by which public housing gets converted into a vehicle for private hands. NYCHA holds the land, but the operating surplus is privatized. These huge institutional landlords need to service their debt and generate returns for their investors, which means the logic governing the building shifts from "house people" to "generate sufficient revenue to cover debt service plus management fees plus profit."

A 2024 Comptroller audit found eviction rates at PACT buildings were significantly higher than at traditional NYCHA developments. Of course they do. Under NYCHA, a tenant who falls behind on rent is dealing with a public bureaucracy that has no profit motive to push them out. Under private management backed by private financing, a non-paying tenant is a threat to the revenue stream that services the debt.

Tenant protections that applied under NYCHA don't automatically carry over either. Some residents have voted against conversion when given the choice.

## The Tension

Mamdani is skeptical of PACT/RAD. He prefers direct public investment, fixing the buildings with public money rather than handing them to private managers. "Public housing is public. Stop bringing in private management to take over," he said during the campaign.

This is ideologically consistent but means finding tens of billions somewhere without private financing. The $80 billion backlog is real. Doubling the city's capital investment from $1.27 billion to $2.54 billion would take over 30 years to clear the backlog, even without new problems arising.

The administration hasn't rejected PACT entirely — the Bay View Houses PACT deal that closed in December 2025 is moving forward with Clean Heat for All heat pumps. But the direction is clear: public investment where possible, skepticism of private conversion, and a focus on quality-of-life improvements that residents can actually feel.

Notably, the NYT ran a piece in February asking "What About Public Housing?" after Mamdani's aggressive moves against private landlords. The implicit critique: it's easier to target negligent landlords than to fix the city's own housing stock. The administration's response so far is the heat pump investment and rhetoric about dignity, but the structural challenge remains.
