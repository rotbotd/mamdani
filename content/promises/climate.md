Mamdani's climate platform centers on building decarbonization, renewable energy expansion, and environmental justice — with the Green Schools plan as the most concrete commitment. Climate was not a major campaign issue (rents, transit, and immigration dominated), but Mamdani enters office with arguably the most significant urban climate law in the country already on the books and struggling for enforcement.

## Local Law 97

The centerpiece. Passed in 2019 as part of the Climate Mobilization Act, LL97 requires buildings over 25,000 square feet to slash emissions 40% by 2030 and reach net-zero by 2050. Nearly 50,000 buildings are covered. When the first phase came into effect in 2024, only about 8% needed immediate upgrades to comply, but over 50% will need changes to hit the 2030 target.

The Adams administration effectively gutted enforcement — expanding compliance extensions, delaying penalties, and introducing Renewable Energy Credits that let building owners buy their way out of compliance rather than actually reducing emissions. Mamdani inherits a law that's ambitious on paper and toothless in practice, with less than four years to hit the first major target.

The political tension is real: LL97's biggest opponents are co-op buildings, condos, and landlord associations who say compliance costs too much. Retrofitting large buildings — replacing gas boilers with electric heat pumps, upgrading insulation, modernizing electrical panels — can cost millions. Those costs get passed to tenants through rising maintenance and rents, which directly conflicts with Mamdani's affordability agenda. His Homeowner Policy Memo acknowledges this explicitly, noting that outer-borough co-ops and Naturally-Occurring Retirement Communities often don't have access to capital for the required renovations.

The administration's approach has three prongs: extending the J-51 tax abatement through 2035 to help buildings finance retrofits (and reducing the per-unit filing fee, which at $85/unit means a 2,800-unit building like Penn South owes $230,000 just to apply), staffing up the NYC Accelerator (currently understaffed and underfunded) to provide technical assistance, and exploring bulk purchasing of heat pumps and other equipment to bring down costs. NYCHA's pilot purchase of 10,000 induction stoves is the model, but scaling that to the broader building stock will require something new — potentially a dedicated authority for bulk procurement.

The fundamental question is whether Mamdani can thread the needle: actually enforce the law (which Adams refused to do) while making compliance affordable enough that it doesn't drive up housing costs. So far there's been more signaling than action on this front.

## All-Electric Buildings Act

Mamdani backed the legislation banning fossil-fuel equipment (including gas stoves) in new buildings. This is separate from LL97 — it applies to new construction rather than retrofits of existing buildings. Less politically contentious since developers building from scratch can design around it.

## Build Public Renewables

As an assemblyman, Mamdani pushed the Build Public Renewables Act, which requires NYPA (the New York Power Authority) to develop renewable energy projects. The goal is public ownership of new renewable capacity rather than relying on private utilities. This matters because NYC depends on fossil fuels for over 90% of its power — electrifying buildings only helps the climate if the grid is clean.

The mayor has limited direct control over the grid, but City Hall plays a role in negotiating contracts for public institutions with NYPA. Governor Hochul directed NYPA in June 2025 to work on building at least 1 GW of new nuclear reactors. Mamdani has called nuclear "something worth exploring" but hasn't taken a strong position on the Indian Point reconstruction proposals.

## Peaker Plants

Mamdani kicked off his political career in 2020 fighting to close gas-fired peaker plant expansion in Astoria, near his Assembly district. These plants — used only during peak demand — are some of the dirtiest power sources and disproportionately affect environmental justice communities. He advocated for shutting down fossil fuel plants near schools.

## Green Schools

The most concrete climate commitment: a $3.3 billion plan to retrofit 500 school buildings with rooftop solar and upgraded HVAC, build 500 green schoolyards (replacing heat-absorbing asphalt), convert 50 schools into year-round resilience hubs, create 15,000 union jobs, and save ~$275 million/year in energy costs.

## Resilience and Adaptation

Climate adaptation is arguably more urgent than decarbonization for New York. Routine rainstorms regularly overwhelm the city's stormwater systems and cause deadly flooding — two men died in basement apartments in Brooklyn and Manhattan during heavy rains in October 2025. The city has been working on adaptation since Superstorm Sandy in 2012, with the Bluebelt Program on Staten Island serving as a testing ground for water-management infrastructure that's now expanding to Brooklyn, Queens, and Manhattan.

The resilience hubs in schools are part of the adaptation strategy — giving neighborhoods cooling centers during heat waves and emergency shelter during storms. But as the Urban Green Council has noted, the city's adaptation efforts are "a little decentralized." Whether Mamdani consolidates and expands them remains to be seen.

## Status

The Green Schools plan has been announced but funding hasn't appeared in the preliminary budget. LL97 enforcement has been signaled but not yet demonstrated through actual penalties. Like most of Mamdani's transformative proposals, the big-ticket items await Albany tax revenue that hasn't materialized. The most actionable near-term steps — staffing up the Accelerator, extending J-51, beginning enforcement — don't require new money and would signal seriousness.
