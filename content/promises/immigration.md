Mamdani signed Executive Order 13 in February 2026 — one of the strongest sanctuary city orders in the country.

## Executive Order 13

The order bars ICE from entering any city property (schools, shelters, hospitals, parks) without a judicial warrant. It prohibits sharing city data with federal immigration authorities. Each agency must appoint a privacy officer within 14 days and audit sanctuary law compliance. An Interagency Response Committee coordinates the response.

The administration launched a "Know Your Rights" campaign distributing 32,000+ multilingual flyers in 10 languages through faith institutions.

## Legal Defense Funding

Mamdani pledged to boost total immigration legal defense funding by $100+ million:

- Rapid Response Legal Collaborative: $500K → $25 million
- Immigrant Family Unit Program: $16M → $30 million  
- Immigrant Opportunity Initiative: $20M → $40 million

He also supported hiring 200 lawyers for the city legal department.

## The Confrontation

Mamdani called for abolishing ICE and confronted ICE acting director Tom Homan directly in Albany.

He's proposed thinking "expansively" about protecting undocumented New Yorkers, referencing de Blasio's precedent of shredding personal documents from IDNYC applications — though this hasn't been repeated in other forums.

## Chief Immigration Officer

In February 2026, Mamdani appointed Faiza Ali, a former NYC Council staffer, as chief immigration officer to coordinate the city's response.

## Migrant Shelters

Mamdani announced plans to close NYC's last emergency migrant shelter by end of year — shifting toward permanent housing solutions rather than the emergency shelter system that expanded dramatically under Adams.

## The Trump Meetings

A surprising element of Mamdani's immigration approach: he met with Trump at the White House twice. At the second meeting, he raised the detention of a Columbia University student and reportedly secured their release. Critics from the left noted the optics of friendly engagement with someone he'd called "a fascist," but the administration framed it as pragmatic advocacy for vulnerable New Yorkers.

Trump called him "very rational."
