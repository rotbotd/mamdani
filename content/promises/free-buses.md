Permanently eliminate fares on all NYC bus routes, including express buses (currently $7.25/ride).

Nearly 50% of bus riders were already not paying because they couldn't afford it, resulting in $315 million in lost revenue. So instead of criminalizing poverty, make the buses free and have the city and state reimburse the MTA.

The cost estimates range from $652 million after accounting for savings on fare enforcement and collection up to approximately $800 million per year. One analysis by Charles Komanoff estimated that eliminating fares would boost ridership by 23% and generate close to $1.5 billion in economic benefits, though the revenue loss may actually be higher than $600 million when you include MTA Bus lines on top of NYC Transit buses.

## Pilot

Mamdani helped launch a free bus pilot as an assemblyman with five routes, one per borough, running fare-free from September 2023 to September 2024. Results:

- 30% ridership increase on weekdays
- 38% ridership increase on weekends
- 38.9% drop in assaults on bus operators

The assault reduction is significant. MTA data shows that since 2019, harassment and assault against bus operators accounts for 63% of all workplace violence against transit workers. Most of these confrontations happen over fare disputes. Eliminate the fare, eliminate the flashpoint.

The Transport Workers Union backs the plan for exactly this reason. "It nearly totally eliminates the assaults on bus operators," said TWU international president John Samuelsen.

## But...

The MTA is a state agency, not a city one. The mayor doesn't control the buses. Hochul does.

This is the legacy of a 1953 decision that put the subway and buses under state control. The city owns the streets and can build bus lanes, but it can't set fares or run service. Any fare-free plan requires Albany's approval and someone to cover the lost revenue.

Hochul has said she's "not ready" for free buses. MTA leadership has expressed concern about the revenue gap. The agency's own evaluation of the pilot noted that speeds didn't increase on free routes and called fare-free rides "the wrong message" when 44% of bus riders already aren't paying.

## The Plan

Phase in free fares borough by borough over four years, funded by corporate tax increases and taxes on high earners. Express buses included.

As mayor, Mamdani is pushing for a free-bus pilot during the 2026 World Cup, so five weeks of fare-free service citywide when global attention is on the city. The idea is to demonstrate the concept at scale and build political momentum.

Mamdani's proposed funding: raising the top state corporate tax rate to 11.5% and adding a 2% NYC income tax on anyone making more than $1 million a year. When pressed on specifics he said "the most important fact is that we fund it, not the question of how we do it, but that we do it."

But "funded by taxes on high earners" means Albany has to pass those taxes. The opposition is predictable. Hochul said she's "not raising taxes on people at a time when affordability is the big issue," which is a beautiful piece of ideological inversion: framing a tax on millionaires and corporations as an affordability problem for regular people. The AEI piece warns that businesses and high-income individuals are "free to move" and many have "already decamped to Florida," which is just the [[promises/rent-freeze#landlords-will-exit-the-market|landlord exit argument]] applied to the tax base. Landlords threatening to leave if you ask it to pay for public goods. Fox called it "socialist" and brought up Soviet grocery stores.

The "how will you pay for it" question always gets posed as if free buses are some exotic novelty, but nobody asks "how will you pay for" free roads for cars. The French city of Dunkirk went fully fare-free in 2018 and it's working. The question isn't really about fiscal feasibility, it's about whether transit is understood as a service for profit or a public utility. Same question as housing. Ugh.

Here's the interesting wrinkle though. His campaign also floated tapping the more than $800 million in unpaid fines on landlords for code violations as a revenue source, which ties the free bus policy directly back to the housing enforcement question. Landlords aren't paying their fines for code violations, the city isn't collecting, and meanwhile tenants are riding buses they can't afford to get to apartments that are falling apart.

## What can Mamdani do?

Build bus lanes. The city controls the streets.

Adams promised 150 miles of new bus lanes and delivered far less. He reversed plans for dedicated lanes on Fordham Road under political pressure and pulled back on the 34th Street busway. NYC buses average 8 mph which are among the slowest in the country.
