Mamdani's criminal justice agenda is built around closing Rikers Island, ending solitary confinement, and expanding alternatives to incarceration. The most symbolically powerful appointment of his administration so far is Stanley Richards as head of the Department of Correction — the first formerly incarcerated person to lead the agency.

## Closing Rikers

Mamdani committed to following through on the plan — passed under de Blasio in 2019 — to close Rikers Island and replace it with four smaller borough-based jails in Manhattan, Brooklyn, the Bronx, and Queens. The city has spent years and billions on this transition, but progress stalled under Adams. The legal deadline for closure is 2027, though virtually no one believes that timeline is achievable.

The new borough jails are designed to be smaller, closer to courthouses (reducing transit time for detainees and families visiting them), and designed around a more humane model than the notoriously violent Rikers complex. The Manhattan facility at 80 Centre Street is furthest along in construction.

The political challenge is that Rikers closure has become a proxy for broader crime-and-punishment debates. Any spike in crime generates calls to abandon the plan. Mamdani has been unequivocal that the closure will proceed, but the timeline remains unclear.

## Stanley Richards

Richards served six years in state prison and five on Rikers before becoming a social worker and eventually Deputy Commissioner of DOC under de Blasio. His appointment as Commissioner is both substantively and symbolically significant — it puts someone who experienced the system's failures in charge of reforming it. He has spent decades working on alternatives to incarceration and reentry programs through the Fortune Society.

Mamdani ordered his Corporation Counsel to develop a plan within 45 days to bring Rikers into compliance with Board of Correction minimum standards. The conditions at Rikers have been the subject of federal monitoring and a consent decree — guards have been indicted, detainees have died, and basic services like medical care and heating have repeatedly failed.

## Solitary Confinement

Mamdani pledged to implement Local Law 42, which limits solitary confinement beyond four hours. The law was passed by the City Council in 2021 but Adams effectively refused to enforce it, and a court challenge from the correction officers' union delayed implementation. The law replaces traditional solitary with "de-escalation" units that allow more time out of cell, access to programming, and limits on how long someone can be isolated.

Correction officer unions have fought this aggressively, arguing that solitary is necessary for managing violence within the facilities. The counterargument — supported by extensive research — is that solitary confinement worsens mental health outcomes and increases violence upon release, making facilities less safe in the long run.

## Alternatives to Incarceration

The administration expanded supportive housing programs for people leaving the criminal justice system. Just Home, a program at Jacobi Hospital, provides 83 apartments for formerly incarcerated people with complex medical needs. The Justice-Involved Supportive Housing program has placed over 350 people in permanent housing.

Mamdani supported maintaining bail reform and opposed rollbacks — a position that put him at odds with centrist Democrats and the police unions during the campaign. He argued that pretrial detention should be based on flight risk, not ability to pay, and that the data shows bail reform did not cause the post-pandemic crime increase.

## The Tension

Criminal justice reform is where Mamdani's progressive base and the general public are furthest apart in polling. His approach — reduce incarceration, close Rikers, end solitary, invest in alternatives — has strong support from reform organizations and the DSA base but generates intense opposition from correction officer unions, tabloid editorial boards, and Republican critics. The Richards appointment drew both praise from reform advocates and attacks from the New York Post, which ran his mugshot.

Whether Mamdani can sustain this agenda through inevitable bad-news cycles — a high-profile crime by someone released under bail reform, a violent incident at a borough jail — will test whether the political coalition that elected him holds.
